General Description

Members of the Carolingian Dynasty had served as mayors of the palace under the Merovingian kings from the late 7th century CE onwards, wielding substantial power behind the throne. In 752 CE, however, Childeric III (last of the Merovingian rulers) was deposed and they seized outright control of the Frankish realm.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_1_FrCarlE][MAJIDBENAM_REF_2_FrCarlE] With the new dynasty the capital moved east: Aachen, or Aix-la-Chapelle, became the main royal residence of the Carolingian monarchs until the empire began to disintegrate in the 9th century.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_3_FrCarlE][MAJIDBENAM_BR]Charlemagne was the most powerful Carolingian king, but after his death in 811 CE, the empire stopped expanding. The year 811 also marked the beginning of a rise in sociopolitical instability that resulted ultimately in a complete split of the kingdom. After the 843 CE Treaty of Verdun, the Carolingian lands were partitioned among Louis the Pious' sons: Charles took the west, Louis the German the east, and Lothair took the Frankish territory between these two regions.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_4_FrCarlE] The Treaty of Meerssen (870 CE) resulted in the absorption of the central Frankish realm into West and East Francia, forming a boundary that even now endures as the border between France and Germany.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_5_FrCarlE] The empire was briefly reunited from 884 to 887 under Charles the Fat,[MAJIDBENAM_REF_6_FrCarlE] but as a rule the Frankish lands remained politically fragmented from the mid-9th century to 987 CE, when power passed to the Capetian Dynasty.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_7_FrCarlE][MAJIDBENAM_BR]This polity represents the early period of Carolingian rule, from 752 to 840 CE.[MAJIDBENAM_BR]Population and political organization[MAJIDBENAM_BR]In the Carolingian era, the lands under Frankish control grew considerably and an administrative system was developed in order to govern this large territory.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_8_FrCarlE] One official position that first appeared in this period was the missus dominicus (king's representative), who could be sent out from the court to inspect the counties and pass on the king's decrees.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_9_FrCarlE] Decrees were sometimes set down in documents called capitularies.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_10_FrCarlE] However, capitularies post-dating 843 CE are only found in West Francia, and they stopped being produced there too after the death of Charles the Bald in 877.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_11_FrCarlE][MAJIDBENAM_REF_12_FrCarlE] This might suggest that the office of missus dominicus did not exist beyond that date and did not survive the rise in instability.[MAJIDBENAM_BR]The Carolingian kings ruled in an essentially decentralized fashion like the Merovingians before them. Control over the regions was delegated to fief holders, often hereditary vassals of the king.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_13_FrCarlE] The king ruled by decree[MAJIDBENAM_REF_14_FrCarlE] and under Charlemagne (r. 800-814 CE) counties were established as the basic unit of governance.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_15_FrCarlE] Counts were responsible for enforcing local laws, dispensing justice and setting taxes.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_16_FrCarlE] By 850 CE, almost every county in West Francia also had a viscount, who assisted the count in his duties.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_17_FrCarlE][MAJIDBENAM_BR]During Charlemagne's reign, the population of Gaul probably reached 5 million[MAJIDBENAM_REF_18_FrCarlE] but levels of urbanization were low in these supposed 'dark ages' of medieval France: no town reached over 10,000 inhabitants between the 8th century and 1000 CE.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_19_FrCarlE]
