General Description

After the collapse of the Wari empire, the Cuzco valley once more underwent a phase of regionalization, known as the Late Intermediate Period (1000‒1476 CE).[MAJIDBENAM_REF_24_PeCuzL2] In other valleys of Peru, this period saw the emergence of complex kingdoms such as the Chimu, the Chincha, the Ischma and the Chanchay.[MAJIDBENAM_BR]In the Cuzco Valley, this period lasted from the early 11th century to the early 15th century and was characterized by incipient state formation in two areas. To the west, the Killke or K'illke may have been the successors to the local Qotakalli chiefdoms of the Early Intermediate period and Middle Horizon.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_25_PeCuzL2][MAJIDBENAM_REF_26_PeCuzL2] Centred around the location of modern Cuzco, where Killke material has been excavated,[MAJIDBENAM_REF_27_PeCuzL2] their sphere of influence extended south of Cuzco for around 20 kilometres.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_28_PeCuzL2] Smaller undefended polities to the west of modern Cuzco may have been under Killke domination, while it is likely that the developing political units to the north were independent.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_29_PeCuzL2][MAJIDBENAM_BR]In the Lucre Basin to the east, another powerful cluster emerged, referred to as 'Pinagua' or 'Pinagua-Moyna' by ethnohistorians. These polities developed around the sites of Choquepukio, Cotocotuyoc and Minaspata.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_30_PeCuzL2] They may have upheld the cultural legacy of Wari colonizers from the Middle Horizon,[MAJIDBENAM_REF_31_PeCuzL2][MAJIDBENAM_REF_32_PeCuzL2] as their ceramics and architecture show profound Wari influence.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_33_PeCuzL2] Choquepukio in particular exhibits significant continuity with the Wari through its monumental architecture.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_34_PeCuzL2] Even further to the east, another small state may have existed around the settlements of Andahuaylillas, Huaro and Urcos. It has been suggested that the Pinagua cluster and these polities together prevented the eastern expansion of the Killke until the late 14th century.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_35_PeCuzL2] Between these two extremities of the Cuzco Valley, the Oropesa Basin acted as a buffer zone.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_36_PeCuzL2][MAJIDBENAM_REF_37_PeCuzL2][MAJIDBENAM_BR]The Killke, centred in the Cuzco region, appear to have been the most influential and expansionary of these cultures during the Late Intermediate Period: their ceramics were widely used, in contrast to the limited spread of Lucre Basin styles.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_38_PeCuzL2][MAJIDBENAM_BR]Population and political organization[MAJIDBENAM_BR]The Cuzco Basin and the Sacred Valley experienced significant population growth after 1000 CE,[MAJIDBENAM_REF_39_PeCuzL2] although population estimates are extremely difficult to produce.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_40_PeCuzL2] The Lucre Basin cluster comprised at least 4 settlements extending over 10 hectares (e.g. Minaspata and Coto-coto), and Choquepukio covered 60 ha. These settlements may have housed several thousand people each.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_41_PeCuzL2] The capital of the Killke polity, now buried beneath modern Cuzco, may have covered 50 ha.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_42_PeCuzL2][MAJIDBENAM_BR]The second half of the Late Intermediate Period proved to be a gradual phase of consolidation for what would become the Inca Empire.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_43_PeCuzL2] Indeed, the Killke followed a pattern of 'leapfrogging', conquering new territories on an ad hoc basis.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_44_PeCuzL2] Administrative and temple buildings at Qhapaqkancha, Markasunay and Pukara Pantillijlla probably developed during the Inca expansion into the Sacred Valley (1250‒1310 CE).[MAJIDBENAM_REF_45_PeCuzL2] One suggested cause of this expansionary strategy is a period of drought between 1250 and 1310.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_46_PeCuzL2][MAJIDBENAM_BR]Meanwhile, there was another phase of construction at the site of Choquepukio around 1300 CE, which may have corresponded to the arrival of a new elite group from the Titicaca cultural sphere.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_47_PeCuzL2] Our evidence for this incursion comes from DNA analysis as well as from the presence of chullpas (burial towers) and ceramic styles characteristic of the Altiplano.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_48_PeCuzL2] The construction of grand niched halls and the presence of luxury items made of ceramic, metal, precious stones, and bone may indicate elite-sponsored feasting.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_49_PeCuzL2][MAJIDBENAM_REF_50_PeCuzL2] The paramount leader of Choquepukio may have held these feasts in order to establish his power in relation to other local elites.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_51_PeCuzL2] Early Inca chronicles document these Pinagua leaders or capacs as a rival force, blocking their expansion into the Lucre Basin.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_52_PeCuzL2][MAJIDBENAM_BR]Archaeological evidence of a major burning event between 1400 and 1430 CE indicates that the Choquepukio state finally succumbed to Killke expansion.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_53_PeCuzL2] Choquepukio's arts and monumental architecture profoundly influenced the material culture of the Inca Empire:[MAJIDBENAM_REF_54_PeCuzL2] for example, Inca ceramics blended Lucre ceramic technology with Killke motifs.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_55_PeCuzL2] With the demise of the Choquepukio polity and the expansion of Killke dominance across the valley, the conditions were in place for the rise of the Inca Empire during the 15th century, creatively building upon the legacy of its predecessors.

♠ Nonwritten records ♣ suspected unknown ♥ "The khipu is most often associated with Inca accounting, but it was borrowed from Andean traditions that were developed at least a thousand years before the Incas. Some scholars think that the earliest systematic use of the khipu as a recording tool occurred in Wari or other expansionist states. It may owe its genesis to institutional demands, such as keeping track of supplies (Quilter and Urton 2002; Brokaw 2010). If they are right, then the Incas elaborated a package of administrative techniques developed by their imperial predecessors. It is certainly the case that the tool was well established across the Andes before the Inca empire came along, as different societies reportedly used a variety of knot-tying conventions. The chronicler Murua wrote that, “each province, as it had its own language, also had a different form and logic [razón] of quipo” (translation from Platt 2002: 229).
