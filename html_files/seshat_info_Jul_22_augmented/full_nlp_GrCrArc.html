Hierarchical Complexity[edit]

♠ Settlement hierarchy ♣ [1-5] ♥ 1-2, 5 levels. Crete is divided into regional city-states which controlled a well defined region. The settlement hierarchy within these states is simple. It was centered upon the city where all the government, public and religious buildings were located and villages and hamlets scatted throughout its rural countryside. City-states were independent of their neighbors and there was a political unity among the urban centre and the rural settlements. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_7_GrCrArc]

Law[edit]

♠ Formal legal code ♣present♥ Codes of laws and regulations were recorded in inscriptions (commonly incised on stone). Legal inscriptions, the oldest are dated to the 7th century BCE, were found so far in the city-states of Gortyn, Axos, and Dreros. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_13_GrCrArc] Many inscriptions were displayed on sanctuaries, a tangible expression of the intersection of law and religion. The texts are fragmentary and provide a very cloudy picture on the legal systems adopted by the Archaic city-states. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_14_GrCrArc] The codes primarily deal with issues of ownership and pledges. The most important inscription, the longest inscription of the Greek world, is the famous Gortyn Code or the Great Code. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_15_GrCrArc] Although the inscription is dated to the first half of the 5th century BCE, it is argued that echoes Archaic legislative systems.
♠ Judges ♣present♥ The council of elders, the Gerousia, whose members were chosen among the best Kosmoi, had legislative and juridical authority. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_16_GrCrArc] Officials appointed by the state, they are called dikastai (δικαστές), acted as judges; they deal mostly with cases involving inheritances and pledges. Special judges, the hetaireai, deal with matters of tribal law and custom, others, called orfanodikastes (ορφανοδικαστές) were appointed to supervise the affairs of orphans or minors, the ksenios Kosmos (ξένιος κόσμος) had important duties connected with the foreigners living in the city, and finally the cosmos hiarorgos (ιαροργός) was responsible for matters related to the religion.
♠ Courts ♣ present♥ Legal disputes were tried in the agora (the central gathering place) of the city.

Information[edit]

♠ Length ♣ present♥ The measurements for the length are based on daktylos (δάκτυλος) for smaller units and pous (πούς) and station (στάδιον) for larger units. The values of these units varied according to area and period but the relative proportions were generally the same: daktylos: 19.3 mm, pous: 0.308 m. and station: 185.4 m. Length was measured by a cord of some standard length (schoinion), a metallic chain (halysis), or a rod from reed or wood (kalamos). [MAJIDBENAM_REF_17_GrCrArc]
♠ Volume ♣ present♥Greeks measured liquid capacity by the metretes (34 liters) and dry capacity by the medimnos (25 mgr). Each measure was based on the common unit of the kotyle. The values of these units varied according to area and period but the relative proportions were generally the same. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_18_GrCrArc]
♠ Weight ♣ present♥ There were two dominant weight standards in the Greek world, the Euboic (introduced to Attica by Solon) and the Aeginetic. The weights, their names bear a nominal resemblance to currency, are the obol (0.72 g in Attic/Euboic and 1.05g in Aeginetic standard), drachma (6 obols: 4.31g in Attic/Euboic and 6.3g in Aeginetic standard), mina (100 drachmae: 431g in Attic/Euboic and 630g in Aeginetic standard), and talent (60 mine: 25.86 kg in Attic/Euboic and 37.8 kg in Aeginetic standard).[MAJIDBENAM_REF_19_GrCrArc] The values of these units show a great variation according to area and period. The Cretan weight system was based on the Aeginetic standard.
♠ Time ♣ present♥ In the Greek world, the day began after sunset. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_20_GrCrArc] The day, divided in units, was measured by sundials (scathe dials or ring dials) and during the night by water clocks (clepsydrae). Cretans named each year after the president of the board of Kosmoi (protokosmos or kosmos o epi poleos). The year, started in the summer solstice, was divided into twelve months: Agyios (December-January), Dioskouros (January-February), Theodosios (February-March), Pontos (March-April), Rabinthios (April-May), Hyperberetos (May-June), Nekysios (June-July), Basileios (July-August), Thersmofhorion (August-September), Hermaios (September-October), Metarhios (October-November), Eiman (November-December).

♠ Ideology forbids slavery for co-ethnics ♣ absent ♥ “I am not aware of any explicit condemnation of slavery as an institution by any ancient philosopher, nor any movement, large or small, to abolish slavery. Both Stoics and Epicureans made much of the idea that ‘true slavery’ is not external but an internal matter: whether one is a slave to one’s desires and emotions. Thus every slave can be ‘free’ and every master can be “enslaved” depending on their character. Even Epictetus, who was a slave himself, did not advocate the abolishment of slavery (nor of course did the Christians). It seems to have been unthinkable to them, given how embedded it was in the social and economic structure of their society. It is true that sanctuaries sometimes owned slaves, just as they owned land or other forms of property. During the Hellenistic period, religious devotion to certain deities was conceptualized as a form of willing slavery; this is known in Ptolemaic Egypt, for example.
