The extent to which the Chinese or lacework elm (Ulmus parvifolia), as opposed to other varieties of trees, was planted to form the palisades cannot be ascertained from the brief historical records of this defensive experiment in reforestation." ♠ Name ♣ ancestor worship ♥ "The living communicated with their ancestors through solemn divination, reports, prayers, and offerings of wine and food. The chief sacrificer had to purify himself in advance through abstinence of varying duration. ♠ Name ♣ palace exams ♥ Civil service exams were the (highly ritualised and symbolically charged) procedure through which Chinese men could become part of the bureaucracy. "The anxiety from preparing for the Keju exams and the disappointment of doing poorly on them led to incidents of pathological behavior. Repeatedly failing the Keju exams took a severe psychological toll on the failed candidates. Effects of failure ranged from mild cases of self-doubt to various psychopathologies and, in some cases, to suicide or physical violence directed at others. In her analysis of classic exam-related novels, Judith Zeitlin found that physicians during the Ming dynasty had identified a specific form of anxiety-depression, called by these physicians siyu and referred to as 'male anxiety' by Benjamin Elman, that is associated with repeatedly failing the Keju exams. [...] What is more, these psychological and behavioral phenomena are not unique to a certain period of time. Rather, cases like those mentioned were documented throughout the history of the exam and were particularly prevalent after the Song dynasty when the exam became available to the general public and even more highly competitive." [MAJIDBENAM_REF_509_CnNSong] "Severe punishments were meted out for people caught cheating, including being caned or being placed in stocks for a month. There were occasional cases of actual executions of cheaters. By the time of the Song dynasty, large prisonlike exam compounds that could accommodate up to 22,000 or more examinees, complete with armed guards and with thorny bushes planted around the outer walls, were built. During the exams, very strict personal search (including body searches) procedures and very strict exam procedures were followed. Candidates were grouped into small groups responsible for each other’s behavior. Watchtowers were built over and dispersed around the exam compounds. Other measures included strict and precise rules and specifications regarding clothing and very detailed specifications of what items could be brought to the exam compounds. Also included were the sequestering of examiners prior to exams, restricting examinee movements during exams, physically separating exam scorers from exam compound administrators and other staff to prevent the influencing of the scorers’ judgment, forbidding the use of any words or phrases in the essays and poems that would reveal an examinee’s identity, and the separation of the responsibilities for exam administration (Department of Rites) and for subsequent official appointments of successful examinees (Department of Personnel) to minimize corruption. These measures were in addition to the widely known practice of covering the examinees’ name to keep scoring anonymous and employing copyists to copy the examinees’ answers and scoring only the copy to avoid the recognition of handwriting. "Yet, in spite of all these measures, cheating was often rampant." [MAJIDBENAM_REF_510_CnNSong]
