Social Scale[edit]

♠ Polity territory ♣ [8,000-10,000] ♥ in squared kilometers.
♠ Polity Population ♣ [10,000-60,000] ♥ People, per Phoenician city-state. Unfortunately, due to the difficulties in excavating the Phoenician cities, there appear to be no good estimates for the populations for each polity. Markoe (2000:196) tentatively suggests that by the 4th Century BCE, Tyre's population "may have reached forty thousand, if one accepts Arrian's testimony concerning the number of soldiers slain and inhabitants sold into slavery at the time of Alexander's siege." Diodorus reported that over 40,000 people in Sidon were killed when Artaxerxes III captured the city in 351 BCE, with an unspecified number of survivors sold into slavery. Yet enough people remained for Sidon to recover as a thriving metropolis within only a few years.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_15_LbAcPho] One might suppose that at least ten thousand Sidonians remained after Artaxerxes' depredations. Tyre and Sidon were, of course, the leading cities of Phoenicia, giving us little to work with when estimating the size of the other cities. Arwad, in the second rank of Phoenician polities, was on an island of some 40 hectares; according to the "conventional" (and questionable) rule of thumb of 250 inhabitants per built-up hectare,[MAJIDBENAM_REF_16_LbAcPho] this would indicate some 10,000 inhabitants. However, classical sources indicate that the island was densely populated, with strong fortifications and an urban core with multi-story buildings.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_17_LbAcPho] We can likely double the initial estimate, in my view.

Limits on Power of the Chief Executive[edit]

♠ Executive power is separate or independent from military ♣ inferred absent ♥ Even at their weakest point, the Phoenician kings directly commanded their militaries. As with many things Phoenician, the information we have on their political system is sketchy. Each city was ruled by a king; however, the travelogue of Wen-Amun discusses an "assembly" at Byblos that governed alongside the king and discussed affairs of state, over which the king presided. A similar assembly was recorded in the 14th-Century-B.C.E. Amarna Letters, where it was called the Council of Elders. In the 7th-century BCE treaty between Tyre and Assyria, the members of the council are called the "elders" of the city. There was also a larger body called the People's Assembly, which apparently "was made up of the 'enfranchised' population of the city, i.e. its free male citizenry." [MAJIDBENAM_REF_96_LbAcPho] Glassman notes that after the Assyrian conquest, the kings' role as military commanders on land was sharply limited; they were however still in command of the Phoenician navies, a role that often took them away from their cities for weeks at a time. In their absence, he infers, the councils of wealthy merchants very likely assumed greater control of state administration and set up a more permanent "republican" structure.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_97_LbAcPho] We have no sense for the powers of the Council of Elders or the People's Assembly, whether they had explicit authority to restrain the king or to propose new laws or repeal existing ones. However, we can perhaps draw some parallels with the governmental structure in independent Carthage. Its constitution was discussed incompletely by Aristotle in his Politics, and he praises its institutions profusely for their democratic achievement.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_98_LbAcPho] Executive power rested in the hands of kings, but they could not act unilaterally. A body called the Hundred and Four was made up of judges; subordinate to them was a Council of Elders, also called the Supreme Magistracy of the Hundred. Legislative authority rested in the People's Assembly, and "anyone who wishes may speak against the proposals introduced," per Aristotle. Officials were chosen both by merit and by wealth; Aristotle reports that the Carthaginians believed that poor men could not govern well or devote themselves to public affairs. Scholars of Carthage also note that outside writers often used the terms "king," "general," "head judge", and others interchangeably, and wonder if there were truly an institution of kingship or if that was an incorrect inference by observers.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_99_LbAcPho] Perhaps, once Carthage was freed from the rule of distant Tyre, they did away with the idea of a preeminent king altogether. Josephus (1st Century CE) also notes that during the invasion of Nebuchadnezzar II in the 6th Century BCE, Tyre lacked a king for seven years, during which time the council of Tyre ruled, and magistrates were popularly elected for short periods. Stockwell argues that by the time of Alexander's siege of Tyre, the power of the council had essentially eclipsed that of the king.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_100_LbAcPho] He assigns to Phoenicia a key role in influencing early Greece in the direction of republican democracy. At any rate, there are good theoretical reasons to suppose that the Phoenician councils had some amount of effective power as a partial counterweight to the king. Ruling a mercantile society with powerful merchants, the regime would have needed some way to encourage cooperation with royal power, and especially with its tax demands, since brute coercion would be unlikely to work well. Thus, there would be strong pressures to grant rights or powers to the wealthy merchant class to some extent, and perhaps extending further to the enfranchised population. One can draw a strong parallel with the governmental structures in the Dutch Republic, a similarly wealthy mercantile empire that featured oligarchic rule by its merchant class and a high degree of political inclusion for the time.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_101_LbAcPho]
