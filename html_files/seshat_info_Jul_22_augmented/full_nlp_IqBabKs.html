[9] The Kassite Dynasty is notable for the unification of Sumer and Babylon to create the Empire. It was marked by large building projects, especially in old Sumerian cities such as Ur, Uruk and Eridu. [10] The Babylonian Empire was at this time secondary to the powerful surrounding states Egypt and Assyria. The Kassites had a reasonably good relationship with Egypt; there were several intermarriages and gifts were given and recieved. On the other side, they constantly fought with Assyria until the Assyrian king Tukulti-Ninurta I captured the Babylonian king, Kashtiliash IV and conquered Babylon. Assyrians ruled Babylon for seven years. Elam also started invading Babylonian territory in the latter Kassite Dynasty, eventually setting Kutir-Nahhunte on the throne in the north, leaving the Kassites surviving in power in the south. Not many years later Kuti-Nahhunte conquered the whole of Babylon, ended the rule of the Kassite Dynasty and took their god, Marduk, to Susa. [11][MAJIDBENAM_BR]The period was characterised by the overall population decline occurring across the Near East. Border towns and villages were abandoned and irrigation became less successful. Much of the administration was undertaken by or in the temples who effectively owned most of the land; however, another type of land ownership developed, which was the land gifted by the king to religious, military and administrative elites. The non-elites, on the other hand, suffered during this period, becoming markedly impoverished as they became unimportant in social government. [12][MAJIDBENAM_BR]In general, the Kassites made only limited changes to Babylonian culture, mostly assimilating into Babylonian society. As such, it can often be difficult to ascribe evidence to the Kassites specifically, as opposed to the ongoing Babylonian empire. [13]

Most dysphoric collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ šar puhi ♥ The Substitute King Ritual. This ritual included also another ritual called 'Bīt Rimki' which was dedicated to of king's purification.[100],[101] 'Bīt Rimki' was probably practiced at the beginning and at the end of the Substitute King Ritual. The several ritual practices were undertaken by the king and the priests in order soften the gods anger caused by king's behaviours. These included both bathing and fumigating to remove the physical and magical impurity. When the king was touched by impurity he was not able to rule properly any longer and various badlucks and catastrophes happened to his people and the kingdom. During the ceremony of king's purification by taking the bath, the 14 prisoners were participated in this act and then they were released and sent to the enemy countries. There was believed that these prisoners absorbed all evil from the king and take with them to the king's enemies. After the ceremony of night recitation of long prayers, the king took a bath again and in seven houses he both performed rites of purification and recited prayers again in the front of figurines. According to Ambos, then "In the fifth house, the exorcist stabbed a figurine of the curse-demon; the king washed his mouth with water and beer, and spat this on the figurine, which was then buried. In the sixth house, figurines of the sorcerer and sorceress were bound with a rope and buried. In the seventh house, a figurine of the death-spirit was buried."[102] The Subsitute King Rituals was practised when 'an eclipse of the moon or sun occurred during which the planet Jupiter was not visible, this was taken as a very bad and inauspicious portent announcing the death of a king.' In order to neutralize these negative effects, the 'false', temporal king was chosen to replace the real king in the time of danger. During that time the substitute king absorbed the evil of the eclipse and the real king was saved.[103] Some written records suggested that šar puhi was practiced both in the Old Babylonian and Middle Babylonian Period [104]
