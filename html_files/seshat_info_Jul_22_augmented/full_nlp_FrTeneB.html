Law[edit]

♠ Formal legal code ♣ inferred absent ♥Honour price was "the equivalent of the Anglo-Saxon custom of wergild, the amount payable by a third party in the event of unlawful injury or death." "The concept of honour price was fundamental to the legal system of the Celts. It dictated the conduct of all judicial cases, since the value of an individual's oath or evidence was determined by his honour price. To bring a lawsuit against someone with a higher honour price required the intervention of a patron of higher rank, creating an environment in which the support of the richest and most influential members of the elite was constantly sought after." [MAJIDBENAM_REF_42_FrTeneB]

Most frequent collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ sanctuary rituals ♥ "At the end of the early Tene (c. 300 BC) the public sanctuaries, yielding evidence of regular and intense sacrificial activity, appeared in the north of Gaul. This event has a considerable sociological significance. "The sacrifices which took place there gathered together human groups that were previously separated, at least in cult matters: priests, warriors, peasants, craftsmen perhaps. They met there regularly, but above all they devoted a particular amount of their normal work to the production of goods that together they consecrated by destroying them. Even if the three agents of Indo-European society - priest, warrior and peasant - did not hold an equal place in these ceremonies, for the first time their goods were put together to form a sacred offering for the benefit of all. "The sanctuary is an indication of a people becoming aware of its unity and its component parts. It reveals the emergence of the notion of citizenship. The citizen participated in the cult, while the outcast was excluded. ♠ Name ♣ initiation hunt ♥ The following excerpt suggests that the hunt was an important activity for the Gauls, and that one of its functions was as an initiation ritual for boys to become men and warriors. It is this specific type of hunt that will be coded here. "Arrian informs us that the Celts never hunted 'without the gods'. By that he meant that the hunt, like the war to which it was related , was a ritual activity that needed divine permission and assistance. "Arrian tells us that hunting was one of the Celts' favourite occupations and not simply a noble pastime. It was a daily level of skill and courage which involved many levels of society. The methods were diverse - on horseback, on foot, with dogs, or with nets. Among the Gauls, hunting became an art for which they remained famous throughout the Roman empire. "[...] "Religion was of course intimately linked with this occupation. To kill an animal had remained, since prehistoric times, a hazardous act that could not be carried out without the consent of the gods, or at least without the ritual precautions that took its place. "There are in effect three ways of putting an animal to death: the hunt, sacrifice, and butchery. The last was the rarest in antiquity. It was therefore in sanctuaries and at the hunt that animals were killed - domestic animals in the first case and wild ones in the second. There is no doubt that this method was the first form of sacrifice. It demanded the same ritual precautions: consent of the victim or the deity thought to represent it, and the reservation of part of the spoils for this same divinity. "The hunt also had a sporting character that was recognized by the peoples of antiquity. Thus the deity of the hunt was often linked with war; this was the case with Artemis in Greece, who was also kourotrophos (one who rears children); the initiation of youngsters was in fact done by means of exercises and hunting trials. In Gaul, where the cultivation of the body never attained the perfection that it knew in Greece, it is probable that the hunt mainly formed part of the apprenticeship of war. A youth had to confront nature and its savage denizens before being admitted into the society of men. "[...] "It is obvious from [a] passage [from Arrian] that the capture of game was regarded as a theft from nature, and one that had to be repaid. Its redemption was made according to a principle dear to the Celts and one that Caesar had already emphasized, that 'only a life can redeem a life'.
