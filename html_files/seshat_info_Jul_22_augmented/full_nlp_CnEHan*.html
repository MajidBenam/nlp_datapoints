♠ Religion ♣ ♥China’s Han dynasty is divided into two periods: Western Han or Former Han (202 BCE-9 CE), and Eastern Han or Later Han (25-220 CE). The period between the two Han dynasties was an interregnum ruled by Wang Mang who overthrow the Han and founded the short-lived Xin dynasty.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_13_CnEHan*] Rulers of the Western and Eastern Han are descended from Han founder Liu Bang.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_14_CnEHan*] The Han dynasty was reinstated when military troops revolted against Wang Mang and attacked the capital of Chang’an in 23 CE.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_15_CnEHan*] The first recognized Eastern Han emperor Emperor Guangwudi moved the capital to Luoyang in 25 CE.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_16_CnEHan*][MAJIDBENAM_BR]Buddhism spread into China during the Eastern Han period. The religion soon began to influence Chinese morals and ethics.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_17_CnEHan*] Han innovation continued into the Eastern Han period. The eunuch Cai Lun invented paper made from mulberry bark in 105 CE.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_18_CnEHan*][MAJIDBENAM_BR]The decline of the Eastern Han was marked by series of natural disasters including floods and plagues beginning in 168 CE.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_19_CnEHan*] The disasters were accompanied by two large peasant uprisings: the Yellow Turban Rebellion and the Five Pecks of Rice rebellion.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_20_CnEHan*] The Eastern Han also faced constant rebellions from Qiang ethnic minorities.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_21_CnEHan*] Provincial warlords aided the central government in suppressing these major rebellions. These warlords eventually became rulers of the provinces.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_22_CnEHan*] Warlord Dong Zhou seized Luoyang in 190 CE but was defeated by the warlord Cao Cao.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_23_CnEHan*] Eastern Han emperors stayed on the throne but the Han empire was split between three generals, ushering in the Three Kingdoms period.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_24_CnEHan*][MAJIDBENAM_BR]Eastern Han territory covered 6.5 million square kilometers in 100 CE, but only 2.5 million square kilometers by 200 CE.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_25_CnEHan*] At its peak, the Han dynasty encompassed modern China, northern Vietnam, Inner Mongolia, southern Manchuria, and parts of modern Korea.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_26_CnEHan*][MAJIDBENAM_BR]Population and political organization[MAJIDBENAM_BR]The Eastern Han continued many of the administrative practices of the Western Han.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_27_CnEHan*] However, the dynasty was marked by bloody political infighting including succession conflicts, and attempts to grab power by consort clans and eunuch cliques.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_28_CnEHan*] Eunuchs had a strong influence in the Eastern Han government and competed with Confucian officials and the imperial clan. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_29_CnEHan*] The decline of the Eastern Han was marked by the rise of strong provincial rulers with independent armies, or warlords, and a weakening of the corrupt central government.[MAJIDBENAM_BR]The population of the Eastern Han was between 48 and 50 million people in 140 CE.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_30_CnEHan*]. Luoyang was home to 420,000 people in 100 CE, but only 100,000 by 200 CE.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_31_CnEHan*][MAJIDBENAM_REF_32_CnEHan*]

Largest scale collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ imperial sacrifice at the capital ♥ NB Though the grand imperial rituals characteristic of previous dynasties (e.g. tours of inspection, as well feng, shan and chiao sacrifices) lapsed during the reign of the first Han Empire (partly due to the polity’s political instability, and partly because some of these rituals were seen as too closely associated with the preceding Qin Dynasty), some of them, including the imperial sacrifice at the capital (chiao), were restored towards the end of the second century BCE. “The chiao ceremony itself was modeled after the rite used at Yung. It began in the evening and lasted until the following dawn. Fires were laid on all the altars, and vessels, and vessels, stands, and cooking utensils were arranged alongside. The principal victim offered to T’ai I was a bull with a long tail. The other offerings of the Yung sacrifice were also used, with the addition of new wine, jujubes and dried meats. The Supreme Emperors received offerings at the same time, but these consisted only of new wine and prepared foods set out in stands and ritual vessels. When the live victims had been killed and ritually presented to T’ai I, they were all cooked. The principal victim, the bull, was stuffed with a deer inside of which a pig had been placed, and all were put in water for the preparation of soup. During the ceremony, the emperor wore yellow robes, while the priest and sacrificer who assisted at T’ai I’s altar wore embroidered clothes of purple. Other priests and sacrificers performed duties at the surrounding altars of the Supreme Emperors. Emperor Wu chose a number of writers to compose songs for the new part of the rites. Nineteen hymns were written [...]. The hymns were sung at the sacrifices by seventy specially selected boys and girls. The singing was accompanied by music played on drums, stone chimes, wind instruments and two types of lute, one with twenty-five strings and another known as a k’ung-hou, both of which had been newly invented for use at the chiao sacrifice. Dances which presumably pantomimed the rods of the hymns were performed to the music by one thousand boys, who are spoken of as being arranged in good order in eight groups, which, it will be recalled, was the number of groups of dancers used during the Eastern Chou period.” [MAJIDBENAM_REF_321_CnEHan*]

♠ Name ♣ ancestor worship ♥ "The living communicated with their ancestors through solemn divination, reports, prayers, and offerings of wine and food. Those in charge of states, fiefs, and families were privileged and obliged to officiate at rites; those not so privileged were expected to assist in whatever capacity was appropriate to them. The chief sacrificer had to purify himself in advance through abstinence of varying duration. At the appointed time, he would enter the consecrated ancestral shrine, bow, prostrate himself, and make offerings of food and wine. He was assisted by a variety of attendants, including those who prepared the food, took charge of the utensils, performed divinations, and pronounced prayers. This impersonator also needed to undergo purification before the rite. During the rite he would drink the wine offered to the ancestors and in trancelike fashion passively allow the spirits to animate him. ♠ Name ♣ Lunar New Year ♥ "In stark contrast to the La festival and the Great Exorcism on the eve of the La, the lunar New Year ceremony in Han times was uncompromisingly secular. There he received his officials, vassal kings who came to court from their estates in the provinces, and, as the Han empire spread to impose its suzerainty upon surrounding areas, representatives and tributary missions from the Hsiung-nu and other surrounding tribes, as well as more distant countries. Then came eating and drinking and merriment accompanied by musical entertainments. In front of the palace there were also spectacular 'fish and dragon' processions. As to how the ordinary people celebrated the lunar New Year, we know almost nothing." [MAJIDBENAM_REF_444_CnEHan*]

♠ Name ♣ blood covenant ♥ "The oldest forms of Chinese oath-making appear to have been oaths of allegiance, specifically the blood covenats of ancient times. Such rites [...] could be performed in a number of circumstances, including forming alliances, resolving conflicts, or determining guilt, with the gods serving not only as witnesses to these rituals, but also as potential punitive agents should the participants fail to keep their word (Lewis 1990; see also Liu 1998; ter Haar 1998: 163, 178, 181). [...] The participants in such rites first purified themselves through fasting. After the composition of a written agreement (zaishu), the killing of an animal victim (referred to as shen) took place at an altar, following which its left ear was placed in one vessel and its blood in another. Blood was then sprinkled on the altar as a means of summoning the gods or ancestors, following which the text of the covenant was read. Each of the participants in the covenant then drank some of the victim's blood, or in other cases smeared the blood on his lips (shaxue). One copy of the covenant was buried in a pit (keng) containing the corpse of the animal victim, while the parties to the ritual also ratified their alliance (limeng). Another copy of the covenant was stored in an archive (mengfu) (Dobson 1968: 271-272). Dobson also cites the use of human blood, including in oaths between lovers to take care of illegitimate children (ibid.: 272-273, 278). "Blood covenants invariably included a contingent curse on those who violated their terms in the form of a 'malediction' (zu).
