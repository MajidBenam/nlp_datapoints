Law[edit]

♠ Formal legal code ♣present♥ Codes of laws and regulations were recorded in inscriptions (commonly incised on stone). Legal inscriptions were found in many city-states and provide a picture on the legal systems adopted by the Cretan city-states. [19]. The most important inscription, the longest inscription of the Greek world, is the famous Gortyn Code or the Great Code. [20]. The inscription consists of 12 surviving columns of text written on the circular walls of a public building, perhaps the bouleuterion of Gortyn. The first fragment of code was discovered in the 1850s and the rest of the inscription was brought to light by Federico Halbherr and Ernst Fabricius. The inscription stones had been reused as part of the foundations of the Roman Odeion (1st century BCE). The text is written boustrophedonically in the Dorian dialect. The Great Code record laws related to inheritance, ownership of slaves, rape and adultery, matters of childhood, divorce and widowhood, and sale and mortgaging of land. The legislation make clear distinctions between the different social classes (free Cretan citizens, serf, slaves and foreigners). The absence of other legislative texts set this inscription aside as a unique source for the study of the economy and society of Cretan cities. The Great Code I. But if he do not release him, let the judge sentence him to a stater for a free man, a drachma for a slave, each day until he has released him. But if he deny that he made the seizure, the judge shall decide with oath, unless a witness testify. If one party contend that he is a free man, the other that he is a slave, those who testify that he is free shall be preferred. But if they testify either for both parties or for neither of the two, the judge shall render his decision by oath. But if the slave on account of whom the defendant was defeated take refuge in a temple, the defendant, summoning the plaintiff in the presence of two witnesses of age and free, shall point out the slave at the temple; but if he do not issue the summons or do not point him out, he shall pay what is written. And if he do not return him, even within the year, he shall pay in addition to the sums stated one-fold. But if he die while the suit is progressing, he shall pay his value one-fold. II. This prestige is partly due to mythological traditions and Minoan memories and survivals. The laws that, according to legend, Minos received every nine years, and the figure of Rhadamanthys the just judge, bear witness to a dim recollection of an earlier rule of law. From as early as the 7th century BCE, Crete had engaged in important legislative innovations, some of which can be reconstructed from the later laws of Gortyn. The famous legal inscription of Gortyn is not an isolated example. Fragments of laws dating from the 7th to the mid-5th century survive in many cities. The reasons for this legislative activity - an activity that includes both the recording of older laws and the introduction of new ones - were the major problems concerning land ownership, inheritance, small landowners' dependence on creditors as a result of the gradual spread of a monetary economy, and the presence of individuals who lacked political rights but engaged in important financial activities as traders, craftsmen and freelance workers. There was very little interest in reform of the existing regime: the handful of laws on civic issues were intended to limit the arbitrary actions and immunity of the Kosmoi.

♠ Weight ♣ present♥ There were two dominant weight standards in the Greek world, the Euboic (introduced to Attica by Solon) and the Aeginetic. The weights, their names bear a nominal resemblance to currency, are the obol (0.72 g in Attic/Euboic and 1.05g in Aeginetic standard), drachma (6 obols: 4.31g in Attic/Euboic and 6.3g in Aeginetic standard), mina (100 drachmae: 431g in Attic/Euboic and 630g in Aeginetic standard), and talent (60 mine: 25.86 kg in Attic/Euboic and 37.8 kg in Aeginetic standard).[26] The values of these units show a great variation according to area and period. The Cretan weight system was based on the Aeginetic standard.
♠ Time ♣ present♥ In the Greek world, the day began after sunset. [27] The day, divided in units, was measured by sundials (scathe dials or ring dials) and during the night by water clocks (clepsydrae). Cretans named each year after the president of the board of Kosmoi (protokosmos or kosmos o epi poleos). The year, started in the summer solstice, was divided into twelve months: Agyios (December-January), Dioskouros (January-February), Theodosios (February-March), Pontos (March-April), Rabinthios (April-May), Hyperberetos (May-June), Nekysios (June-July), Basileios (July-August), Thersmofhorion (August-September), Hermaios (September-October), Metarhios (October-November), Eiman (November-December).

♠ Expert ♣ Kostis Christakis ♥General Description of Warfare: The 5th century BCE was a period when Crete was torn apart by violent internal wars. [35] The picture of the internal hate was described by Plato in Laws. He said that "What people call peace, in Crete is simply a word, as an undeclared war is the natural reality of the cities of Crete". Τhe rivality between powerful city-states was a constant source of tension, either between them or among weaker city-states. The main cause of antagonism was the need for territorial expansion, a need that was particularly pressing for cities whose economy was based on agropastoral production. Conflicts intensified from the end of the Archaic period (end of the 6th century BCE), when land began to be concentrated into a few hands and demands by landless farmers for its redistribution intensified. The expansionist tendencies of Knossos, the most powerful city-state on the island, led to many military conflicts. Its primacy is discerned in many treaties - that with Tylissos in 450 BC, for instance - setting out the terms of joint military operations and distribution of spoils. City-states from the rest of Greece also participated in the disputes, either as arbiters (Argos) or in support of a particular Cretan city (Sparta). Archaeological evidence of contemporary military technology in Crete is very limited; however, the Cretans had undoubtedly adopted a military technology similar to that of the city-states of Classical Greece. [36] On the other hand, we do have a wealth of information on citizens’ training and martial skills. Strabo, mentioning the work of Ephorus of Cyme, reports that: “With respect to the form of government, which Ephorus has described at large, it will be sufficient to give a cursory account of the principal parts. The law-giver, says Ephorus, seems to lay, as the foundation of his constitution, the greatest good that states can enjoy, namely, liberty; for it is this alone which makes the property of every kind which a man possesses his own; in a state of slavery it belongs to the governor, and not to the governed. The liberty also which men enjoy must be guarded. Unanimity ensues, when the dissensions that arise from covetousness and luxury are removed. Now where all live temperately and frugally, neither envy, nor injuries, nor hatred have place among equals. Whence the young were enjoined to repair to the Agelae, and those of mature age to assemble at the Syssitia, or common meals, called Andreia, in order that the poorer sort, who were fed at the public charge, might partake of the same fare as the rich. With a view that courage, and not fear, should predominate, they were accustomed from childhood to the use of arms, and to endure fatigue. Hence they disregarded heat and cold, rugged and steep roads, blows received in gymnastic exercises and in set battles. They practised archery, and the dance in armour, which the Curetes first invented, and was afterwards perfected by Pyrrhichus, and called after him Pyrrhiche. Hence even their sports were not without their use in their training for war. With the same intention they used the Cretan measures in their songs; the tones of these measures are extremely loud; they were invented by Thales, to whom are ascribed the pæans and other native songs and many of their usages. They adopted a military dress also, and shoes, and considered armor as the most valuable of all presents." [37] Cretans were brave warriors and famed archers. The difficult economic situation of the island led many young men to offer their services as mercenaries in foreign armies especially after the Peloponnesian War.

Most euphoric collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ Ekdysia or Periblemata ♥ Ekdysia or Periblemata is essentially a single ritual. [132] The ceremony combined elements of fertility, initiation and marriage-ritual and clearly have roots in the earliest stratum of Cretan religion. For the youth were apparently initiated into manhood, citizenship and marriage at the same period of life. When growth was achieved, the boy died as a boy and was born again as a man, cast aside his boyhood costume and wore mans clothes. The ritual, took place during the spring, culminated in a festival in which the young men put of the garments of ephebic age (Ekdysia) and received the garment of the citizen (Periblemata). The ritual was followed by solemn oaths, promising to protect their city, defend their allies and fight agains their enemies, feasts, and collective marriages. The organization of these rites was on the responsibility of the state institutions. It is very like that these rites goes back to the Bronze Age period.[133]

♠ Ideology forbids slavery for co-ethnics ♣ absent ♥ “I am not aware of any explicit condemnation of slavery as an institution by any ancient philosopher, nor any movement, large or small, to abolish slavery. Both Stoics and Epicureans made much of the idea that ‘true slavery’ is not external but an internal matter: whether one is a slave to one’s desires and emotions. Thus every slave can be ‘free’ and every master can be “enslaved” depending on their character. Even Epictetus, who was a slave himself, did not advocate the abolishment of slavery (nor of course did the Christians). It seems to have been unthinkable to them, given how embedded it was in the social and economic structure of their society. Traditional Greco-Roman religion had nothing doctrinal to say about slavery as a social institution, so far as I can tell; in practice it was accepted and not regarded as problematic. It is true that sanctuaries sometimes owned slaves, just as they owned land or other forms of property. During the Hellenistic period, religious devotion to certain deities was conceptualized as a form of willing slavery; this is known in Ptolemaic Egypt, for example. People would ‘enslave’ themselves for a period to gods like Sarapis.” [209]
