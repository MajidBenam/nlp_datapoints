Law[edit]

♠ Formal legal code ♣ inferred absent ♥Customary law?[MAJIDBENAM_BR] Honour price was "the equivalent of the Anglo-Saxon custom of wergild, the amount payable by a third party in the event of unlawful injury or death." "The concept of honour price was fundamental to the legal system of the Celts. It dictated the conduct of all judicial cases, since the value of an individual's oath or evidence was determined by his honour price. To bring a lawsuit against someone with a higher honour price required the intervention of a patron of higher rank, creating an environment in which the support of the richest and most influential members of the elite was constantly sought after." Largest scale collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ enclosure rituals ♥ "Archaeology makes the first impact of a truly Celtic religion felt at the end of the early Iron Age, or Hallstatt period. In fact the first structures that could be considered as cult sites date from this epoch. "In many cases their religious function is no more than the most plausible hypothesis. These structures are in fact enclosures, most often found in the midst of cemeteries. In general, cult remains (offerings or sacrificial debris) are not forthcoming to support the interpretation. Nonetheless, a few peculiarities allow them to be distinguished from strictly funerary structures. They are very large enclosures, of elongated shape and almost uniformly rectangular. They do not contain any graves, but their internal space is taken up by postholes and big stones, to which at present an astronomical function is ascribed. By casting the sun's shadow on the ground or somewhere else, these reference points would have permitted the establishment of a calendar or proof of an astronomical event (the solstice for example). Cremation areas are fairly often found in the enclosures and betray activities using fire. "Two of these enclosures deserve particular mention. The first is that of Aulnay-aux-Planches in the Marne. Some 90 metres long and 15 to 20 metres wide, it is oriented towards the north-east, with its entrance opening towards the south-east. It contains all the features that have been listed: five stones are arranged on the two short sides, and a post faces the entrance. There was a cremation place; moreover, two pits have been discovered, each containing a human skeleton. "The example of Libenice in Czechoslovakia is more revealing still. Of the same shape and sizes as Aulnay-aux-Planches, it differs markedly in terms of its internal structures. These consist essentially of an enormous pit 15 metres long, 10 metres wide and nearly 2 metres deep. This contained a megalith and two postholes and was itself surrounded by four pairs of postholes. The meticulous excavation and study of the construction plans of these features suggest that this structure enabled astronomical observations that determined the dates of religious feasts. There would have been one feast per year, at the end of October or beginning of November. On this occasion animals were sacrificed, some of whose bones have been recovered from the pit. Before each sacrifice the bottom of the structure was prepared: a layer of earth was spread out. These preparations allow the number of sacrifices to be counted--about 24. This true sanctuary seems to have been destroyed by fire. "[...] "The proximity of these sacred places to burials demonstrates that at this period cult and funerary matters were still intimately linked. There has been an attempt to explain this phenomenon in terms of the cult of heroes, but it seems difficult to conceive of heroes rubbing shoulders with the ordinary dead. In Greece, where their cult was widely developed, the tombs of heroes or their cenotaphs were clearly set apart from cemeteries, whether isolated in the countryside or placed on the outskirts of towns. Moreover, if we really are dealing with this type of cult, it has left neither representation nor inscription nor offering. These enclosures are more likely to be sites of a chthonic cult which was naturally sited among the dead. Ancestors in fact had a privileged relation to the infernal divinities and to do homage to the former was to serve the latter. This close association between the dead and the gods retained something of the primitive belief that it was the buried dead who enriched the earth with their flesh. The ceremonies that took place there were crude, of an agricultural and popular nature." [MAJIDBENAM_REF_111_FrTeneA] For a more conservative view: "[Aulnay-aux-Planches] is an important site, indicating that some kind of ritual associated with the dead went on in the early Hallstatt period, inside a closed space. It evidently included animal sacrifice, since bones of various mammals were found, including dog, fox and young boar, and perhaps human sacrifice also. It is not easy, however, to establish the exact sequence of graves in the enclosure, and we can make no definite assumptions as to the kinds of rites that took place there, or to what supernatural powers those taking part may have addressed themselves. We cannot say whether the enclosure was in use at regular festivals or in times of crisis, or for the funerals of important people. We do not know if the graves it contained were those of people of high rank, or whether some represent human sacrifice. Here we are dependent on informed guesses, based on what the archaeological evidence suggests, and on what we expect of the religion of the early Celts. "Comparisons have been made between this site and another at Libenice near Kolin in Czechoslovakia, this time an oval enclosure surrounded by a ditch, excavated in 1959 (Filip 1976:83ff.; see Figure 1b). Inside, near the centre, was the grave of a woman, buried with elaborate jewellery of about the third century BC. At the eastern end were pits which seem to have been dug out more than once, possibly for libations, as well as pairs of post-holes. At the foot of what must have been two large posts were two neck-rings of bronze, which could have hung on these or been worn by wooden figures set up there. There were also animal bones, and the graves of four other people, including a child. In addition, there was a standing stone, roughly pyramidal in shape, a pit shaped rather like a keyhole, and stone blocks which may have formed an altar. This has led to suggestions that here we have the grave of a priestess of the La Tene period, that idols with torcs round their necks stood in the enclosure, and that human and animal sacrifices took place there, while a skull among the finds was used for libations. "Once again, however, there are extensive gaps in our knowledge. It is difficult to decide on the date of the enclosure, or how long it was in use; an existing sacred place may have been utilised by those who buried the woman there with careful funeral rites. We suffer from the lack of other excavated sites for comparison, although a considerable number of possible ritual enclosures in Celtic are not known and one can hardly make a direct link with the site at Aulnay-aux-Planches when so many centuries divide the two. All that may be safely concluded is that there were enclosed places - and often the position of these is worth studying - where some kind of ritual activity and animal sacrifices took place which may have been associated with the dead. The hope is always that such scattered sites may be matched with further discoveries of a similar kind, so that the gaps in the puzzle grow smaller."[MAJIDBENAM_REF_112_FrTeneA]
