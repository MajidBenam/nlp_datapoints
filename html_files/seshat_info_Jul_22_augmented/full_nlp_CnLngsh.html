[MAJIDBENAM_BR]"It is argued that Taosi controlled a three- (Liu and Chen 2012, p. 221) or four-tier (He 2013) settlement hierarchy in the area between the Fen River and the Kuai River. A recent study counted 54 Taosi-period sites in this region; at least 3 of them, not including Taosi itself, are more than 100 ha in size, 23 are between 10 and 99 ha, and the rest are smaller (He 2013). Regardless of the exact number of hierarchical tiers (which in any case may be impossible to determine based on current data), the range in size, the more-or-less even distribution of the largest sites, and the association between labor investment and the largest site (Taosi) do suggest the development of a regional settlement hierarchy and the ability of the center(s) to recruit labor and accumulate resources." A complex society could not exist without a sophisticated calendar that is primarily founded on the cognition of the four seasons. At the Taosi observatory the observation point, the observation gaps and the Ta’ershan mountains in the distance would have formed a sort of large measuring apparatus we can call a ground “reference point calendar.” The seasons can be determined by watching the sunrise above the Ta’ershan mountains on the midline of one of 11 observation gaps. We understood this after making our own observations at the Taosi observatory from December 21, 2003 to December 23, 2005. Beginning from the winter solstice at observation gap 2 the sun is being gradually followed to the summer solstice at observation gap 12 and back again to the winter solstice at observation gap 2, completing the annual solar cycle and dividing it into 20 segments. Therefore the Taosi people recognized and could observe the changing of the seasons (He 2007). [...] As mentioned earlier, in 2002 a rod of lacquered wood was discovered in the middle-period royal grave IIM22. After we restored it, we saw that the complete length is 187.5cm. It is lacquered in alternating black and green segments that are separated by pink stripes. My research shows that this rod of lacquered wood is part of a device for the measuring of shadows, like a sundial (He 2009c). According to our calculations, different colored markings were used to determine the onset of the seasons, such as the summer solstice. We have calculated the shadow lengths associ- ated with these points, and we are comparing them to figures mentioned in early historical records from later periods." [62]

"The Akha have a transegalitarian society (i.e. between egalitarian and chiefdom).
