♠ Name ♣ Jubilee ♥ Pope Boniface VIII held a Jubilee in 1300, which may have been based on the 1220 Canterbury Jubilee.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_205_ItPapHM] The Jubilee offered plenary pardon for sins for pilgrims visiting Rome's cult centers. It was a smashing success: the Florentine chronicler Giovanni Villani estimated that 200,000 pilgrims in Rome for the Jubilee.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_206_ItPapHM][MAJIDBENAM_REF_207_ItPapHM] While Villani's number, like those of all medieval chroniclers, is a estimate, it indicates the widespread popularity of the ritual. There were enough pilgrims in the city that we have several accounts of people being crushed and trampled to death at particularly popular sites.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_208_ItPapHM] Pilgrims visited the major cult centers around the city, offering gifts; there is a notorious scene in an anonymous chronicler's account in which two clerks stood outside St. Peter's Beyond the Walls with large rakes, gathering up the coins offered by pilgrims.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_209_ItPapHM]"The medieval Roman Jubilee was a year-long celebration of penitence during which pilgrims visited Rome's most important churches and gained plenary indulgences. From its inauguration in 1300, the Holy Year developed through the combined interests of late medieval popes, Roman citizens, and pilgrims. Popes sought to amplify Rome's importance in an era of over-confident kings and emperors; Romans battled for their economic welfare and for ownership of the papacy itself; and pilgrims beset by war and pestilence searched for the complete spiritual cleansing that the Jubilee's plenary indulgence offered. "According to the legend recorded by Cardinal Jacopo Stefaneschi, the Holy Year initially drew its inspiration from a Hebrew tradition recorded in Leviticus in which every fifty years the Jews remitted each others' debts. Stefaneschi wrote of how in 1300 a multitude of millenarian-inspired pilgrims thronged to Rome. In response, Boniface VIII's advisors devised a Christian version of the biblical jubilee. On February 22, the pope declared that each one-hundredth year (centesimo) Rome would host a year of penitential pilgrimage. During this solemn celebration, Christians who visited the Roman shrines dedicated to the church's foremost Apostles, Saints Peter and Paul, would be awarded a plenary indulgence. This 'grand pardon' was a thorough remission of the temporal debt a Christian acquired through sinning. The general understanding was that it promised immediate admission into heaven upon death provided that the pilgrim sinned no further. As with all indulgences, the plenary depended upon the Treasury of Merits earned by Christ and the saints, made visible in Rome's multiple relics. Previously, the plenary had been available only to crusaders. At the time, Boniface was embroiled in conflict with the aggressive King Philip IV of France. Offering Jubilee pilgrims the plenary indulgence highlighted the penitential significance of Rome's relics of Peter and Paul, as well as the pope's central authority in spiritual matters. "To gain the Jubilee plenary, a pilgrim was required to have sincere contrition, perform the sacrament of confession, and visit St Peter's basilica and the basilica of St Paul Outside the Walls for a specified term of days -- fifteen for foreigners, thirty for Romans. The Florentine chronicler Giovanni Villani attested to seeing the peaceful throngs of pilgrims from all over Christendom, 'with much patience, and without noises or quarrels,' crowding Rome's streets, all intent on penitential devotion. The biggest attraction was the Veronica Veil, or Sudarium, an ancient cloth imprinted with the 'true likeness' (vera icona) of Christ and housed at St Peter's." [MAJIDBENAM_REF_210_ItPapHM]

♠ Name ♣ liturgical processions ♥ Religious festivals and saints' day feasts were crucial community events, and festivals structured the year.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_361_ItPapHM] There was a rough framework for the various feasts and saints' days that marked the calendar: weekly holy days, annual moveable feasts, and annual fixed feasts.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_362_ItPapHM] The theology of saints' feasts was being worked out during this period, focusing on the importance of the saints as intercessors before God.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_363_ItPapHM] A saint's feast-day involved Mass and the holy Office, in which prayers were offered to the saint.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_364_ItPapHM] Saints' day commemorations varied dramatically in terms of rituals conducted, but in general, some sort of mass was said, followed by feasting and revelry in the community; Bartlett has observed that "for many, the important point would be that it [a saint's day] was a day off work."[MAJIDBENAM_REF_365_ItPapHM] Drinking, dancing, and general merry-making accompanied the saints' days; for example, in Bologna in the 1260s, the city authorities tried to restrict street-parties because they had become too boisterous, but they had to content themselves with simply restricting parties to places that weren't churches.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_366_ItPapHM] "Liturgical processions" is intended as an umbrella term involving commemorations of saints' feast days, supplicatory processions, and other religious holidays such as the Easter Vigil.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_367_ItPapHM] (Wickham 2015, 321-23) for a discussion of liturgical procession in Rome and our twelfth-century sources for the liturgy These were celebrated all over Christendom, marking universal holidays such as Easter and Christmas, and localized celebrations of patron saints' days.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_368_ItPapHM] (Wickham 2015) is an excellent recent analysis of liturgical processions in the city of Rome for the period 900-1150, although his emphasis is on the better documented 11th and 12th centuries.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_369_ItPapHM] Celebrations would have varied by region and city, but they were always treated as civic holidays. Most dysphoric collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ self-flagellation ♥ Between the thirteenth and the fourteenth century, usually on the occasion of an epidemic or other traumatic event, crowds of people would leave their homes to join long processions, visiting town after town, praying and whipping themselves, believing that through pain they could persuade redeem humanity and bring an end to war and disease. At its peak, this movement attracted up to 10,000 people throughout Europe, though it was disapproved by the authorities, both secular and religious, who threatened flagellants with the death penalty and excommunication. Official disapproval stemmed from the movement's pacifist tendencies. "Con tal nome sono designate sette religiose, diffuse nell'Europa dal sec. XIII al XV, e caratterizzate appunto da un'intensa e continua mortificazione del corpo mediante flagellazione eseguita pubblicamente: accanto all'idea che la flagellazione sia mezzo penitenziale (S. Pier Damiani, S. Domenico il Corazzato) affiora la convinzione che sia anche un mezzo espiatorio per impetrare dal cielo la pace e la cessazione dei malanni come la peste e la guerra. I più noti, e forse i primi, sono i flagellanti che si diffusero, verso la metà del sec. XIII, nell'Italia centrale e settentrionale, a Perugia (1260), Roma, Bologna, Parma, ecc. Folle intere di gente, uomini e donne, vecchi e giovani, si unirono ai primi propagatori del movimento, abbandonando le proprie case per formare lunghe processioni, che andavano da una città all'altra, fra preghiere e battiture, seguendo il clero inferiore che portava croce e sacre insegne. L'esaltazione mistica del sanguinoso rito finì col far credere ai flagellanti che la fustigazione operata direttamente, senza mediazione di sacerdozio, avesse da sola la virtù di dare la salvezza all'infuori dei riti carismatici della Chiesa. Il loro numero salì indubbiamente a cifre molto elevate (qualche decina di migliaia di persone); il movimento assunse allora anche un certo aspetto politico, in quanto i flagellanti, predicando la penitenza, predicavano anche l'abbandono delle contese di partito e la pacificazione nelle città; perciò esso divenne così poco accetto ai governanti, che Uberto Palavicino minacciò la forca ai flagellanti che muovevano verso il Piacentino. L'opposizione dei governi e della Chiesa, preoccupata di un simile movimento in cui effettivamente cominciavano a infiltrarsi vecchi residui delle precedenti eresie, pose così rapida fine al flagellantismo. "Ma una vivace ripresa di esso si ebbe a mezzo il sec. XIV, dopo la grande pestilenza che desolò l'Europa. Questa volta, le bande dei flagellanti iniziarono il loro cammino nell'alta Germania, diffondendosi però ben presto in tutta l'Europa centro-occidentale, e anche in Italia; e questa volta erano irreggimentati secondo norme più precise e più minute di quanto non fosse stato nel secolo precedente. Nel 1333 il beato Venturino da Bergamo condusse a Roma un pellegrinaggio di flagellanti, contenendolo però entro lo schema penitenziale; nel 1348-50 in occasione appunto della peste nera vi fu un nuovo pellegrinaggio; e così nel 1379, nel 1392, nel 1399 per la peste e per il terrore dei Turchi. "Ma anche questa volta la setta ebbe effimera vita. Papa Clemente VI intervenne sin dal 20 ottobre 1349, ordinando di arrestare e imprigionare i flagellanti. XV, nell'Aragona e specialmente nella Turingia e nella Bassa Sassonia (1414), dove si complicò con l'adesione a taluni dei postulati di Wycliffe; e qua e là ancora apparvero flagellanti anche dopo la metà del secolo." Normative Ideological Aspects of Slavery[edit]

♠ Doctrine forbids slavery for co-ethnics ♣ absent ♥ “The scholastics upheld the theoretical positions on slavery laid out by the canonical understanding of Aristotle as expressed by Augustine. The institution was in essence a manifestation of punishment for the evils of the postlapsarian state. It was but a small cognitive step from there to the concept of the master-slave relationship as a reflection of the dichotomy between the righteous and sinners. The generalization of this concept from the individual sinner, or slave, to entire populations seemed entirely natural to theologians and jurists. Aquinas elaborated Aristotle’s notion that slavery, even if devised by man, was in accordance with natural law (‘’Summa contra Gentiles’’, 3), and he supported his position by emphasizing the obvious usefulness of slaves to society (‘’Summa theologiae’’). Bonaventure asserted that this social institution was a reflection of divine order. Those who were more intelligent were thought to be natural masters, while those less intellectually capable but more physically robust found their logical niche in a subservient condition. This was, of course, an exceedingly convenient perception and carried with it the added advantage to slaveholders of being able to see themselves in a positive, even benevolent, light inasmuch as their power over those they owned provided a corrective to the slaves’ innate tendency towards sinfulness. The apparent responsibility to serve as the spiritual guide of the slave, however, brought inherent contradictions. The introduction of non-Christian peoples into Italy was often accompanied by their baptism before sale, making the church’s position on the ownership of Christians even more problematic. This difficulty was generally shrugged off with the repetition of stock sophisms. The conventional baptism was in fact frequently regarded as a mere formality and received little credence. Franco Sacchetti, for example, compared th validity of such conversions to the baptism of oxen and argued for a perpetual state of servitude without the possibility of becoming real Christians (‘’Sermoni evangelici’’, 29).” [MAJIDBENAM_REF_538_ItPapHM]
