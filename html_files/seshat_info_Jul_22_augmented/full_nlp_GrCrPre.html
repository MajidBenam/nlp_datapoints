Hierarchical Complexity[edit]

♠ Settlement hierarchy ♣[1-2]: 3100-2200 BCE; [1-3]: 2200-1900 BCE♥ levels. 3100-2200 BCE: Small nucleated villages and isolated hamlets coexisted throughout the island, especially in lowland and coastal areas. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_7_GrCrPre] The size of these sites is about 2 ha aside Knossos where a settlement of 5 ha already existed since the Final Neolithic period. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_8_GrCrPre] This settled scape is characterized by a considerable degree of regionalism which is expressed in material culture and social practices. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_9_GrCrPre] From the Early Minoan II onwards (2700-2200 BCE), the importance of coastal sites considerably increased while many inland sites seems to be abandoned. Monumental constructions appeared for the first time at Knossos, Malia, Phaistos, Tylissos, and Palaikastro. Many sites were destroyed or burned at the end of the period and this has been interpreted as the outcome of conflict between different social groups aspiring to political and economic power. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_10_GrCrPre] 2200-1900 BCE: Settlement hierarchy change: nucleated villages seems less important and there is a new emphasis on mountain zones. Knossos, Malia and Phaistos increased considerably and become centers of a significant importance. The central monumental construction suggest the presence of a authority controlling the surrounding hinterland. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_11_GrCrPre] These large centers and their supporting surrounding regions each form complex social, political and economic landscapes, in which larger regional-scale integrations could occur. Largest scale collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ secondary burial rites ♥ Despite the wealth of excavated data, it is very difficult to say which ritual exactly was the largest collective ritual of the official cult in Prepalatial Crete, but it does seem that burial rites were particularly important at this time, and attracted large numbers of people. During the Prepalatial period, the practice of group burial was adopted, in vaulted structures or burial buildings imitating houses, or in burial caves. This practice survived, to a greater or lesser extent, throughout the Minoan era. The dead, who probably belonged to the same kinship group, were placed in successive burial layers along with precious personal items, sometimes high-status objects, and ritual vessels containing offerings, indicating afterlife beliefs. From time to time, offerings to the dead were made with incense, and the bones of older burials were moved aside to make way for new ones. Gradually the practice appeared of placing the dead in clay larnakes (sarcophagi) inside the communal tombs, while particular attention was given to the cult of the dead, who were identified with the sacred ancestors of the community. Three-dimensional clay groups of figurines depicting a circle dance in honour of the dead, offerings made by relatives at altars in front of the dead ancestors, and kneading bread for the ritual offerings to the dead, hint at the extent of the mortuary practices used in the cemeteries. Incorporated into the daily life of local communities, the vaulted-tomb cemeteries continued in use during the following periods of the Old and New Palaces, as gathering-places during burial ceremonies and commemorative rites. Paved courts, sacred enclosures, altar-like structures, eating and drinking vessels for funerary banquets, all make up the landscape of death and reveal the burial practices that survived unchanged until the end of the Minoan era. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_38_GrCrPre] "Prepalatial and Protopalatial Crete is characterised by a remarkable persistence of extramural collective burial grounds (of which the tholos tombs12 and house tombs13 are the most conspicuous examples) where secondary burial was universally practised. To date no Prepalatial or Protopatial primary burial has been securely identified. From the longevity of tomb use and the presence of disarticulated human bones in quantities, it is assumed that a good proportion of the population received an archaeologically visible burial in long used family or clan-based tombs, but this assumption needs to be confirmed or rejected by the publication of relevant osteological assemblages. The visual encounter with a mass of disarticulated bodies in the confined space of above-ground built tombs, or rock shelters or caves, must have been striking for the small communities of the period, regardless of whether they were considered ancestors or not, following John Barrett or James Whitley respectively. Sevi Triantaphyllou's work on the human bones from Archanes Tholos Gamma and Moni Odigitria has demonstrated for the first time that the complete decomposition of the body's soft parts was not a necessary prerequisite for the performance of secondary burial. This practice confirms the transient nature of the individual after burial, in contrast to the likely emphasis on staging snapshot identities (through assorted material culture) during primary burial. While evidence for fumigation (charred bones and fire discolouration on walls, deposition of a limestone or soil layer) is convincing, proposals for the manipulation of select bones (through chopping and grinding) should await a supporting osteological study. There is however clear evidence for the curation of the accumulated bones with an emphasis on skulls and long bones, disposed in specially built structures or natural formations, recently confirmed by the exclusive use of the Ayios Charalambos cave for secondary burial." Most widespread collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ secondary burial rites ♥ Despite the wealth of excavated data, it is very difficult to say which ritual exactly was the most widespread collective ritual of the official cult in Prepalatial Crete, but it does seem that burial rites were particularly important at this time. During the Prepalatial period, the practice of group burial was adopted, in vaulted structures or burial buildings imitating houses, or in burial caves. This practice survived, to a greater or lesser extent, throughout the Minoan era. The dead, who probably belonged to the same kinship group, were placed in successive burial layers along with precious personal items, sometimes high-status objects, and ritual vessels containing offerings, indicating afterlife beliefs. From time to time, offerings to the dead were made with incense, and the bones of older burials were moved aside to make way for new ones. Gradually the practice appeared of placing the dead in clay larnakes (sarcophagi) inside the communal tombs, while particular attention was given to the cult of the dead, who were identified with the sacred ancestors of the community. Three-dimensional clay groups of figurines depicting a circle dance in honour of the dead, offerings made by relatives at altars in front of the dead ancestors, and kneading bread for the ritual offerings to the dead, hint at the extent of the mortuary practices used in the cemeteries. Incorporated into the daily life of local communities, the vaulted-tomb cemeteries continued in use during the following periods of the Old and New Palaces, as gathering-places during burial ceremonies and commemorative rites. Paved courts, sacred enclosures, altar-like structures, eating and drinking vessels for funerary banquets, all make up the landscape of death and reveal the burial practices that survived unchanged until the end of the Minoan era. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_64_GrCrPre] "Prepalatial and Protopalatial Crete is characterised by a remarkable persistence of extramural collective burial grounds (of which the tholos tombs12 and house tombs13 are the most conspicuous examples) where secondary burial was universally practised. To date no Prepalatial or Protopatial primary burial has been securely identified. From the longevity of tomb use and the presence of disarticulated human bones in quantities, it is assumed that a good proportion of the population received an archaeologically visible burial in long used family or clan-based tombs, but this assumption needs to be confirmed or rejected by the publication of relevant osteological assemblages. The visual encounter with a mass of disarticulated bodies in the confined space of above-ground built tombs, or rock shelters or caves, must have been striking for the small communities of the period, regardless of whether they were considered ancestors or not, following John Barrett or James Whitley respectively. Sevi Triantaphyllou's work on the human bones from Archanes Tholos Gamma and Moni Odigitria has demonstrated for the first time that the complete decomposition of the body's soft parts was not a necessary prerequisite for the performance of secondary burial. This practice confirms the transient nature of the individual after burial, in contrast to the likely emphasis on staging snapshot identities (through assorted material culture) during primary burial. While evidence for fumigation (charred bones and fire discolouration on walls, deposition of a limestone or soil layer) is convincing, proposals for the manipulation of select bones (through chopping and grinding) should await a supporting osteological study. There is however clear evidence for the curation of the accumulated bones with an emphasis on skulls and long bones, disposed in specially built structures or natural formations, recently confirmed by the exclusive use of the Ayios Charalambos cave for secondary burial." Most dysphoric collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ secondary burial rites ♥ Despite the wealth of excavated data, it is very difficult to say which ritual exactly was the most dysphoric collective ritual in Prepalatial Crete, but the fact that secondary burial rites involved manipulating human remains that had not fully decomposed does suggest some degree of dysphoria. During the Prepalatial period, the practice of group burial was adopted, in vaulted structures or burial buildings imitating houses, or in burial caves. This practice survived, to a greater or lesser extent, throughout the Minoan era. The dead, who probably belonged to the same kinship group, were placed in successive burial layers along with precious personal items, sometimes high-status objects, and ritual vessels containing offerings, indicating afterlife beliefs. From time to time, offerings to the dead were made with incense, and the bones of older burials were moved aside to make way for new ones. Gradually the practice appeared of placing the dead in clay larnakes (sarcophagi) inside the communal tombs, while particular attention was given to the cult of the dead, who were identified with the sacred ancestors of the community. Three-dimensional clay groups of figurines depicting a circle dance in honour of the dead, offerings made by relatives at altars in front of the dead ancestors, and kneading bread for the ritual offerings to the dead, hint at the extent of the mortuary practices used in the cemeteries. Incorporated into the daily life of local communities, the vaulted-tomb cemeteries continued in use during the following periods of the Old and New Palaces, as gathering-places during burial ceremonies and commemorative rites. Paved courts, sacred enclosures, altar-like structures, eating and drinking vessels for funerary banquets, all make up the landscape of death and reveal the burial practices that survived unchanged until the end of the Minoan era. [MAJIDBENAM_REF_122_GrCrPre] "Prepalatial and Protopalatial Crete is characterised by a remarkable persistence of extramural collective burial grounds (of which the tholos tombs12 and house tombs13 are the most conspicuous examples) where secondary burial was universally practised. To date no Prepalatial or Protopatial primary burial has been securely identified. From the longevity of tomb use and the presence of disarticulated human bones in quantities, it is assumed that a good proportion of the population received an archaeologically visible burial in long used family or clan-based tombs, but this assumption needs to be confirmed or rejected by the publication of relevant osteological assemblages. The visual encounter with a mass of disarticulated bodies in the confined space of above-ground built tombs, or rock shelters or caves, must have been striking for the small communities of the period, regardless of whether they were considered ancestors or not, following John Barrett or James Whitley respectively. Sevi Triantaphyllou's work on the human bones from Archanes Tholos Gamma and Moni Odigitria has demonstrated for the first time that the complete decomposition of the body's soft parts was not a necessary prerequisite for the performance of secondary burial. This practice confirms the transient nature of the individual after burial, in contrast to the likely emphasis on staging snapshot identities (through assorted material culture) during primary burial. While evidence for fumigation (charred bones and fire discolouration on walls, deposition of a limestone or soil layer) is convincing, proposals for the manipulation of select bones (through chopping and grinding) should await a supporting osteological study.
