The latter had taken the island from the Byzantines (who subsequently lost the control of the maritime trade routes in the Aegean specifically and the eastern Mediterranean generally), and made it into an emirate, with Khandax, i.e. modern-day Heraklion, as its capital. The surviving evidence for the organization of the emirate and the Cretan society of the period is, unfortunately, scanty, with almost no archaeology and few, contradictory written sources. The original Byzantine sources are biased against the Arabs, and quite a few modern scholars have taken these sources at face value, presenting the Arabs as uncivilized barbarians, overly concerned with piracy. Most widespread collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ salat al jum a ♥ salat al-jum'a We are coding as the collective ritual one particular salat - Friday prayer. As Muslims, scholars, merchants and rulers would have been expected to perform salat regularly. Salat is "the ritualized, obligatory prayer performed five times a day: sunrise, noon, afternoon, sunset, and evening. Salat, which means 'to bow, bend, or stretch,' is composed of a series of yogic movements that include standing, bowing, rising, sitting, turning east and west, and falling prostrate, all repeated in cycles, and accompanied by specific verses from the Quran. "As with all Muslim rituals, salat can begin only after the intention to pray is voiced, and only while the Muslim faces toward Mecca, the direction of prayer, or qiblah. Although salat can be performed individually as a means to purify and cleanse the soul, it is meant to be a communal act that binds the Ummah as a single body. For this reason, it is always preferable to perform salat in a place of assembly. Indeed, one particular salat--the noon prayer on Fridays (salat al-jum'a)--must be performed in the mosque, in the presence of the community. And while five daily prayers may seem like a heavy burden, the obligation is suspended for the sick, those who are traveling, or anyone else who is unable to perform them for any justifiable reason; if one wishes, missed salats can always be made up at a later time" [MAJIDBENAM_REF_98_GrCrEmr]. Most frequent collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ salat al jum a ♥ salat al-jum'a We are coding as the collective ritual one particular salat - Friday prayer. As Muslims, scholars, merchants and rulers would have been expected to perform salat regularly. Salat is "the ritualized, obligatory prayer performed five times a day: sunrise, noon, afternoon, sunset, and evening. Salat, which means 'to bow, bend, or stretch,' is composed of a series of yogic movements that include standing, bowing, rising, sitting, turning east and west, and falling prostrate, all repeated in cycles, and accompanied by specific verses from the Quran. "As with all Muslim rituals, salat can begin only after the intention to pray is voiced, and only while the Muslim faces toward Mecca, the direction of prayer, or qiblah. Although salat can be performed individually as a means to purify and cleanse the soul, it is meant to be a communal act that binds the Ummah as a single body. For this reason, it is always preferable to perform salat in a place of assembly. Indeed, one particular salat--the noon prayer on Fridays (salat al-jum'a)--must be performed in the mosque, in the presence of the community. And while five daily prayers may seem like a heavy burden, the obligation is suspended for the sick, those who are traveling, or anyone else who is unable to perform them for any justifiable reason; if one wishes, missed salats can always be made up at a later time" [MAJIDBENAM_REF_135_GrCrEmr]. Unfortunately, the sources do not say whether the way salat was performed in this polity was in any way different from the preceding description, but it is reasonable to infer that it was broadly similar.

Most euphoric collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ Id al Fitr ♥ 'Id al-Fitr is the best loved Islamic festival, and it was already an important part of the Islamic calendar by the seventh century CE [MAJIDBENAM_REF_172_GrCrEmr]. Descriptions of the festival are surprisingly rare, especially as regards how it was celebrated in the past, but given that most Islamic practices demonstrate a great degree of continuity with the past [MAJIDBENAM_REF_173_GrCrEmr], so descriptions of how the festival is celebrated in modern times may apply to how it was celebrated in the past as well. 'Id al-Fitr is celebrated at the end of the month-long Islamic fast known as Ramadan. "As in the Friday afternoon congregational prayer of the weekly Day of Assembly, the Eid morning prayers at the mosque should be preceded by a bath and attended with clean (often new) clothes. After breakfast, men, women, and children gather at an outdoor prayer meeting, the musallah, in front of a white wall adorned with a niche, the mihraab pointing in the direction of Mecca (like the one in every mosque). Women are given the option of praying at home--as they mostly do the rest of the year. The prayers consist of two units (rakat) plus the six-to-sixteen additional takbir recitations of Allahu Akbar ('God is Most Great'). These set them apart from Friday prayers, as does the fact that they are followed instead of preceded by a two-part sermon (khutba). [...] This enthusiasm erupts spontaneously on the aptly named Eid-al-Fitr, the festival of the breaking of the month-long Ramadan fast. Still, by then, passions have been so well trained that few unbridled excesses are in evidence at the happy family celebrations of warm community that follow or at the fairground attractions that are set up in some places [...]. Indeed, restored social harmony is on display everywhere over up to three days, with people greeting and congratulating each other in the streets as well as on afternoon visits to relatives, friends, and sick people. Many gifts are offered, particularly to the children of less fortunate families. At home, the family celebrates around a noontime festive meal [...]. Dead relatives are also remembered as their graves are visited that day." [MAJIDBENAM_REF_174_GrCrEmr] There is also a specific type of charity given on the occasion of 'Id al-Fitr, known as Zakat-ul-Fitr [MAJIDBENAM_REF_175_GrCrEmr].
