Presumably these rituals were lead by an individual, e.g. an elder.[MAJIDBENAM_BR]"Ashmounds marked the locations of important Neolithic places (e.g. settlements and camps) as well as commemorating and memorializing communal ritual; their permanence and scale further suggest that these highly visible structures were intended to mark the landscape for generations (Allchin, 1963; Boivin, 2004; Johansen, 2004; Paddayya, 2001). The centrality and high visibility of many ashmounds within Neolithic settlements and camps suggests that the ritual practices that created these features were open, public, and socially integrative, transmitting socio-symbolically charged information concerning community integration and social reproduction (Boivin, 2004; Johansen, 2004). The repetitive ritual practices that created these monuments employed a valued and almost certainly sacralized substance (dung) collected from an animal with profound symbolic, economic, and political importance to Neolithic agro-pastoral communities. Ashmound ritual also appears to have produced very specific sets of social claims to places such as seasonal and permanent settlements, adjacent pasturage, water sources, and critically to community cattle herds whose daily movements and seasonal transhumance navigated established and contested spaces within a dynamic Neolithic social landscape. In doing so, ashmound production clearly and legibly socialized places, materializing social relations at community and regional scales of practice. Ashmound production was, then, both ritual and political activity, affirming and reinforcing social relationships by inscribing them in monumental places with a highly visible and legible political materiality within the Neolithic landscape."

Largest scale collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ ashmound ritual ♥ "Ashmounds were the largest and most prominent cultural features produced by Neolithic agro-pastoral communities [...] These large mounded features are composed of stratified deposits of vitrified, carbonized, and decomposing cattle dung mixed with layers and lenses of other culturally modified sediments, artifacts, and faunal remains" [MAJIDBENAM_REF_70_InDecNL]. Boivin [MAJIDBENAM_REF_71_InDecNL] offers the best summary on the theories regarding the ritual significance of these ashmounds, with a particular focus on the views of Allchin [MAJIDBENAM_REF_72_InDecNL]: "Put briefly, Allchin argued that the ashmounds were the result of repeated cycles of dung accumulation followed by burning events that sometimes involved enormous conflagrations (hot enough in some cases to melt ash into a glassy slag; experiments by Zeuner suggest that this occurs at 1200-1230°C: Zeuner 1960). The fires were clearly intentional, for had they not been (but were, say, due to accidental ignition or some sort of spontaneous combustion), we would expect to find them in other periods and in other regions of India, since the accumulation of cattle dung for various purposes is and long has been a common practice across the whole of the subcontinent (Allchin 1963). Presumably also, Neolithic herders would have altered their practices if dung mounds were accidentally catching fire, but instead we find repeated fires at the same locale, and many hundreds (if not thousands) of ashmounds in the Southern Deccan region (Allchin 1963). While Allchin recognized that the burning of rotting stockades and accumulated dung may have had a practical value, perhaps in fighting cattle diseases, he nonetheless emphasized primarily a ‘non-practical, religious purpose’ for the Neolithic dung fires (Allchin 1963, 175). In doing so, he drew upon various lines of evidence, but particularly ethnographic evidence suggesting an association between cattle, dung and fire in seasonal festivals across the Indian subcontinent. Citing extensive examples from the ethnographic literature, he observed that, in particular: '... we find in many parts of southern and central India connections of bonfires with cattle and their movements to the grazing grounds, no less than with fertility. We frequently find too that masses of cow-dung form the focus of the rites. All this is extremely suggestive of the way in which the ash fires came to be ignited and the ashmounds formed' (Allchin 1963, 176). Also ‘suggestive’ Allchin argued, was vibhuti, the ‘sacred ash’ used in Hindu rituals, which was traditionally made from burnt cow dung and could be linked, archaeologically and linguistically, to the ashmounds".

Most frequent collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ men's rituals ♥ Its abundance at key sites in the NGA (most notably, Kupgal Hill) suggests that the most frequent ritual in Neolithic North Karnataka involved the production of rock art. It is likely that rituals surrounding rock art sites were exclusive, as suggested by the fact that pictures are placed on walls that are difficult to access. And, specifically, it seems likely that these were men's rituals, as suggested by the preponderance of ithyphallic figures, not to mention rock art sites' association with sites dedicated to rock quarrying, a likely male activity. These rituals probably included rhythmic rock percussion, as suggested by distinctive marks on some of the rocks, as well as the acoustic qualities of both these rocks and the landscape. And, finally, these rituals probably concerned cattle in some way, as suggested by the fact that cattle are depicted even more frequently than ithyphallic figures. Based on all these things, a few more aspects of these rituals may be inferred, though tenuously. All data from Boivin (2004),[MAJIDBENAM_REF_108_InDecNL] unless otherwise indicated.
