[MAJIDBENAM_BR]The Qing Dynasty (or Empire of the Great Qing, Great Qing, Manchu Dynasty, Manchus, Jin, Jurchens, Ch'ing Dynasty) was China's last imperial dynasty. The founders of the Qing were descendants of Jurchen Jin rulers. The dynasty was founded by Nurhaci and then led by his son Huang Taiji, but did not become an imperial Chinese dynasty until after Huang Taiji's death.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_7_CnQingE] In 1644 CE, Qing forces captured the Ming capital at Beijing from rebels and held a funeral for the last Ming emperor to symbolize Qing inheritance of the Mandate of Heaven.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_8_CnQingE][MAJIDBENAM_BR]The Qing faced conflict with rebels and loyalist Ming forces for the next two decades.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_9_CnQingE] Ming generals who surrendered were given power over large territories in southern China in exchange for loyalty to the Qing. In 1673 CE, leaders from three major southern feudatories led by Wu Sangui rebelled against Emperor Kangxi when he tried to reduce their power.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_10_CnQingE] The Revolt of the Three Feudatories, as this episode is known, lasted eight years.[MAJIDBENAM_BR]We divide the Qing Dynasty into two, an Early period (1644-1796 CE) and a Late period (1796-1912 CE). The division is marked by a period of internal turmoil as well as foreign incursions into its territory and economic sphere. In the Early Qing period, China had been prosperous under Kangxi and Qing rule, but by the time of the Opium Wars in the Late Qing, Western technology and industry had surpassed that of China.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_11_CnQingE] The fall of the Qing Dynasty in 1911 gave rise to the Republic of China.[MAJIDBENAM_BR]Population and political organization[MAJIDBENAM_BR]The Qing ruled over an expansive empire, and its bureaucracy was more efficient than that of previous periods.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_12_CnQingE] Qing rulers adopted the Chinese bureaucratic system first used in the Han and Tang Dynasties.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_13_CnQingE] Before conquering the Ming, the Qing managed its population through a system of hereditary military organizations called the Eight Banners.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_14_CnQingE] These became part of the administrative structure of the Qing Dynasty and were only open to those of Manchu descent.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_15_CnQingE] In the later Qing period, however, the Eight Banners lost some of their political functions and served to enhance the prestige of the top Qing nobility.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_16_CnQingE][MAJIDBENAM_BR]The central government was headed by the emperor and included a 'Grand Council', created by the Yongzheng emperor and expanded by the Qianlong emperor.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_17_CnQingE] The Grand Council ruled over the central ministries and provided a way for the emperor to circumvent the official bureaucracy for many decisions.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_18_CnQingE][MAJIDBENAM_BR]The Qing provincial government consisted of governors who controlled a hierarchical system of officials, prefects, county chiefs, country magistrates, and clerks.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_19_CnQingE] In the early Qing years, provinces were ruled by high ranking officials who were typically of Manchu descent.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_20_CnQingE][MAJIDBENAM_BR]The territory of the Qing empire was more than double that of the Ming.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_21_CnQingE] Tibetans, Uighurs, Muslims, a number of Mongol groups, Burmese, Thais, and indigenous Taiwanese were incorporated into the Chinese empire.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_22_CnQingE][MAJIDBENAM_BR]Three Qing emperors - Kangxi (1662-1722 CE), Yongzheng (1723-1735 CE), and Qianlong (1736-1795 CE) - are historically known as great rulers. During their reigns, China was extremely prosperous.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_23_CnQingE] Qianlong is famous for leading ten military expeditions, including campaigns in Taiwan, Burma, Vietnam and Nepal.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_24_CnQingE][MAJIDBENAM_BR]Based on Chinese census and registration counts, the population of China in 1749 CE was about 177.5 million.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_25_CnQingE] The following century was one of extremely rapid population growth, and by 1851 the population had reached 431.9 million people.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_26_CnQingE] Historian James Z. Most dysphoric collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ palace exams ♥ Civil service exams were taken at four progressive levels: local exams; provincial exams; metropolitan exams; and palace exams. One could not apply for palace exams without having passed metropolitan exams, for metropolitan exams without having passed provincial exams, and for provincial exams without having passed local exams. All types of exams took place in prison-like compounds designed to curb cheating, all four involved questions regarding Confucian classics, poetry and national policy issues, and all four caused heightened anxiety in candidates [MAJIDBENAM_REF_409_CnQingE]. The palace exam has been chosen as the most dysphoric simply because its stakes were highest. "The exams were state occasions. Held triennially, the jinshi event constituted a high point in the capital's calendar with thousands participating, thousands more providing tutorial support, sustenance and encouragement, and yet more working as invigilators, scrutineers and assessors. To preclude cheating, each candidate was installed in his own wooden cubicle. His desk by day served as his couch by night, and for better supervision the lines of cubicles lay open along one side, like a row of latrines. Responsibility for the exam rested not with the Ministry of Personnel but the Ministry of Rites, and jinshi literally meaning 'presented shi', it included a final viva in the presence of the emperor himself. Once again, ritual underlay the whole system. Scholars, just like tribute-bearers, supposedly conformed to the cosmic pattern of attraction, being drawn into the imperial orbit by its sheer centrality. Their attendance affirmed its legitimacy and their distinction added lustre to its brilliance." [MAJIDBENAM_REF_410_CnQingE] "The anxiety from preparing for the Keju exams [which resulted in a jinshi degree] and the disappointment of doing poorly on them led to incidents of pathological behavior. Repeatedly failing the Keju exams took a severe psychological toll on the failed candidates. Effects of failure ranged from mild cases of self-doubt to various psychopathologies and, in some cases, to suicide or physical violence directed at others. In her analysis of classic exam-related novels, Judith Zeitlin found that physicians during the Ming dynasty had identified a specific form of anxiety-depression, called by these physicians siyu and referred to as 'male anxiety' by Benjamin Elman, that is associated with repeatedly failing the Keju exams. In the general literature, there have been many recorded cases of problems of delusions, illusions, and insanity attributed to failing the Keju exams repeatedly." [MAJIDBENAM_REF_411_CnQingE]

♠ Corruption ♣ widespread ♥ It was estimated a magistrate’s annual expense varied from five to ten thousands taels, including salaries paid to private secretaries, food, daily expenses, and entertainment. These expenses were far beyond the magistrate’s nominal salary to cover. The magistrate could meet these expenses by conducting “objectionable practices” or customary fees (lougui). Members of the Chinese bureaucracy tried to collected fees from every imaginable occasion to cover their expenses. Although the fees sounded to be low and irregular, it had become widely accepted and authorized. It was within the tolerance of the law and within the custom of society. But in reality, there was often no distinction between collection of customary fees and corruption. The system had been remained the standard practice to the end of the dynasty. At the provincial levels, there were some attempts made to regulate the practice by listing fees that were permissible. Nevertheless, they never succeeded simply because all levels of officials depended on customary fees which could be charged in numerous ways. The central government had never made any attempt because it hesitated to increase the tax rate. An increase in the tax rate sounded anti-Confucian as the Chinese classical explicitly advocated low taxation to reduce burdens of the Chinese people. The Manchu government used this cultural character to control Han Chinese people because any concrete corruption would not be perceived as a systematical corruption of the government.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_517_CnQingE]

In the yahang system inherited from the Ming dynasty, local brokers had to pay for licenses (yatie) issued by the central government. Licenses were in theory the only middlemen authorized to arrange sales for certain widely used commodities- which even varied from regions to regions- generally including grain, wine vegetables, cloth, and livestock. Yahang charged small commission fees, customarily 5 % or less of the value of merchandise, divided between buyer and seller. The yahang system provided possibilities for corruption and abuses. Yahang enjoyed monopoly privileges in local markets, as they could in effect fix prices. Licensed brokers were, theoretically speaking, accountable to the state for the rates they charged, and the token tax payments they remitted.
