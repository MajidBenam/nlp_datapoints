Hierarchical Complexity[edit]

♠ Settlement hierarchy ♣ 4 ♥ 1-4 The Knossian state was disintegrated in independent regional entities (quasi-polities) centered upon a large town. [15]. The size of these towns varies from 4 to 54 ha. Some were densely occupied while habitation in others was more dispersed, suggesting a trend toward a ruralization of the urban setting. Data points to a four-tiered hierarchy: the large town is surrounded by villages, hamlets, and farmhouses. The most important centers were Kydonia (Chania), in west Crete and Hagia Triada, in south-central Crete. [16] The reference to a wanax (king) in the inscription painted on the shoulder of some inscribed stirrup jars, produced in the area of west Crete, prompting the speculation for the presence of a "palatial" authority. [17] By the end of Late Minoan IIIB most settlements had suffered destruction or abandonment.
♠ Administrative levels ♣ 4 ♥ 1-4 The only centre which provided evidence for Linear B administration during the Late Minoan IIIB period is Kydonia (Chania). It is very likely that administration was organized as in the Mycenaean states. The supreme leader of the state was the king (wanax). [18] The reference to a wanax (king) in the inscription painted on the shoulder of some inscribed stirrup jars, produced in the area of west Crete, prompting the speculation for the presence of a "palatial" authority. [20] Below these leaders were the hequetai, followers, who accompanied military contingents and may also performed other functions. Other officials, the so-called collectors, were involved in acquiring and distributing exchange commodities. Most euphoric collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ opiate consumption rituals ♥ "From the second half of the 14th century BCE onwards, a typical Minoan religious symbol of fertility was the bear-breasted figurine of the goddess with the raised arms. The goddess of poppies, one of the best known of these figurines, presents the surest and most ancient evidence concerning the poppy, the extraction of opium, and the use of both not only in Crete but in the whole of the Greek world (Figure 3). This 78-cm height terracotta figurine bears on its head three movable hairpins shaped as beautiful, well-slit poppy capsules, called “mekones,” the poppies of Papaver Somniferum [6]. The goddess was found in 1937 in Crete in the secret room of a rural cult house from the Late Minoan III period (1300 - 1250 BCE) [7]. The poppy capsules that adorn the head- dress of the goddess have five to six vertical notches (Figure 3, top), which suggest that Cretans knew the method of extracting opium, by incising the unripe seed capsules of the poppy plant to gather the sap, at least since 1250 BCE [5,8]. Their methods of harvesting opium were not much different from the methods used today of circular carving [3,5]. Especially impressive are the folds in the cheeks giving a smiling effect and the lifelessness of the parting of the lips, giving her the appearance of being in a state of torpor induced by opium [5]. For this reason the archaeologist who discovered her proposed that she should be called “the goddess of ecstasy” [7]. Several scholars have argued that opium was probably used in the cult ceremonies of the goddess with upraised arms either for its healing abilities or for the purpose of inducing a state of ecstasy [3,5]. It is noteworthy that a tubular vase discovered at the same site and belonging to the equipment of the goddess is proposed that it was used for preparing inhalations of opium [7]. The finding of the remnants of a heap of coals on the ground of the room, where the goddess was found, provides further evidence that the opium was taken by the inhalation of vapours probably for psychotropic purposes [7]." [130]

Most dysphoric collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ lion hunting ♥ Hunting emerges as an important ritual activity during the Postpalatial Period [148]. In particular, there is some evidence suggesting that the hunting and/or subjugation of lions, and the use of seals depicting these activities, was a key way in which elites advertised their power and prestige. It is worth noting that lions were not present in prehistoric Crete, or, at least, they were not native to the island: instead, it seems likely that, starting from the Neopalatial Period onwards, aristocrats either imported lions from foreign lands or hunted the animals while travelling to the foreign lands themselves. "Depictions of lions in the Final Palace period tend to be seen as more conventional, with more formal poses and designs. Younger (1985: 51-2) suggests that the later lion depictions were hardly based on observation. One argument against this is provided by a sealing from Knossos which, as Rhyne (1970: 186) observed, shows a ‘hair star’ on the shoulder of the lion (Plate 6b): although this could be an observation imported from the Near East, nevertheless it points to a continuing interest in the details of lion morphology (Arkell 1948; Bate 1950; Kantor 1947). The ‘Master of Animals’ depictions are typical of the Final Palace period, most often featuring lions either side of a central figure. Bloedow has argued that, rather than seeing such arrangements as symbolic, they can be interpreted as depicting lion subjugation: ‘Clearly, this is not a fate which lions would have chosen for themselves, and so depicts in supremely dramatic fashion the sheer violence by which they had been brought into subjection’ (Bloedow 1996: 1163-4). Like hunting scenes, these seals can be seen as providing information about the practices involving lions; these later depictions make explicit the practice of subduing a dangerous animal, and so can be seen, like other depictions of lions, as indexes of a dangerous activity. [...] Even if lions were brought to Crete, however, depictions continue to show them as dangerous predators, attacking other animals. The mane is the key to lion depictions throughout Minoan seal engraving, and one can see these depictions as increasingly providing information about the animal whose mane it was. Often the information was incorrect, in comparison with modern zoological knowledge about lions, but this is not always a useful standard against which to judge these depictions. To dismiss these differences simply as poor artisanship is to overlook the fact that they can be seen as useful indicators of what the seal engraver and seal owner knew about lions. Later seals displayed the affordances of the lion as a dangerous animal to be hunted. The seal itself afforded the user of the seal a way to demonstrate knowledge of an unfamiliar animal and associate themselves with the animal practice of lion hunting. Yet these seal depictions reveal varying levels of familiarity with lions." [149]

"Depictions of lions in the Final Palace period tend to be seen as more conventional, with more formal poses and designs. Younger (1985: 51-2) suggests that the later lion depictions were hardly based on observation. One argument against this is provided by a sealing from Knossos which, as Rhyne (1970: 186) observed, shows a ‘hair star’ on the shoulder of the lion (Plate 6b): although this could be an observation imported from the Near East, nevertheless it points to a continuing interest in the details of lion morphology (Arkell 1948; Bate 1950; Kantor 1947). The ‘Master of Animals’ depictions are typical of the Final Palace period, most often featuring lions either side of a central figure. Bloedow has argued that, rather than seeing such arrangements as symbolic, they can be interpreted as depicting lion subjugation: ‘Clearly, this is not a fate which lions would have chosen for themselves, and so depicts in supremely dramatic fashion the sheer violence by which they had been brought into subjection’ (Bloedow 1996: 1163-4). Like hunting scenes, these seals can be seen as providing information about the practices involving lions; these later depictions make explicit the practice of subduing a dangerous animal, and so can be seen, like other depictions of lions, as indexes of a dangerous activity. [...] Even if lions were brought to Crete, however, depictions continue to show them as dangerous predators, attacking other animals. The mane is the key to lion depictions throughout Minoan seal engraving, and one can see these depictions as increasingly providing information about the animal whose mane it was. Often the information was incorrect, in comparison with modern zoological knowledge about lions, but this is not always a useful standard against which to judge these depictions. To dismiss these differences simply as poor artisanship is to overlook the fact that they can be seen as useful indicators of what the seal engraver and seal owner knew about lions. Later seals displayed the affordances of the lion as a dangerous animal to be hunted. The seal itself afforded the user of the seal a way to demonstrate knowledge of an unfamiliar animal and associate themselves with the animal practice of lion hunting. Yet these seal depictions reveal varying levels of familiarity with lions." [175]
