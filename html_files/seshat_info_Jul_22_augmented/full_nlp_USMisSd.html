♠ Name ♣ warrior purification ritual ♥ Chemical traces of caffeine-rich emetic known as the “Black Drink” in the ethnohistoric literature found on Mississippian beaker vessels dating as far back as 1050-1250 CE [MAJIDBENAM_REF_231_USMisSd], some of which are decorated with images of warriors and warfare [MAJIDBENAM_REF_232_USMisSd]. According to Crown et al [MAJIDBENAM_REF_233_USMisSd], Buchanan [MAJIDBENAM_REF_234_USMisSd], and Dye [MAJIDBENAM_REF_235_USMisSd], these are sufficient grounds to infer that, like Native American cultures encountered by European colonialists in the Eastern half of North America in later centuries, prehistoric Mississippian warriors purified themselves before and after battles or raids by consuming this substance. Again, based on both ethnographic parallels and the recovery of serving vessels alongside the Black Drink beakers, these purification rituals probably also involved feasting and/or fasting, and they almost certainly strengthened group solidarity (Buchanan 2014, 274). The idea we have of this ritual may be further fleshed out thanks to the following passage summarising ethnohistorical accounts concerning the indigenous peoples of Southeastern North America: "In addition to being a stimulant, the beverage also acts as a diuretic, causing increased perspiration. And the Indians sometimes used it as an emetic. On these occasions they would drink it in large quantities, and in a quarter to half an hour they would vomit. Sometimes they would hold their arms across their chests and expell [sic] the contents of their stomachs six or eight feet. The Timucuans would drink repeatedly, vomiting it up each time (fig. 56). [...] Caffeine stimulates the central nervous system, exciting it at all levels. [...] It enables a person to have a more rapid and clearer flow of thought, makes him capable of more sustained intellectual effort, and sharpens his reaction. It also increases his capacity for muscular work and lessens fatigue. Moreover, some evidence suggests that large doses of caffeine speed up blood clotting. These effects are pronounced with doses of 0.5 to 1.0 grams--the equivalent of three to six cups of strong coffee. When consumed in large quantities, black drink could have delivered at least this much caffeine, and perhaps as much as 3.0 to 4.0 grams. These effects from large quantities of black drink could have been important and even decisive factors in activities such as the ballgame or warfare."
