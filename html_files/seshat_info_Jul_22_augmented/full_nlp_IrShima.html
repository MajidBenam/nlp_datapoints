According to some researchers, this might have stimulated Shimashkian to formation their own state, but real autonomy was achieved later, during the reign of Ibbi-Sin and rebellion of Ishbi-erra, ensi of Isin, and disintegration of Ur III Empire.[22] [23] There is also believed that Shimashkian had important contribution in collapse of Ur III as they took a part of coalitions against Ibbi-Sin, together with some people of the Zagros (e. g. ♠ Length ♣ present ♥ Liverani notes the so-called "urban revolution" in the Uruk phase 3800-3000 BCE lead to systematic use of measurement systems but that "Systems of measurement (weight, mass, length and area) already existed. They were predominantly based on body parts: the thumb (inch), cubit or foot for linear measurements, or the talent (the weight of a person), or the weight of a donkey and so on." These units "were standardized and linked to a numeral system. In Mesopotamia, the system was sexagesimal, based on multiples of six and ten. Therefore, multiples and divisors of a given unit were 60 and 360, 1/60 and 1/360 and so on." In Uruk phase development could be linked to the emergence of large-scale activities of great organizations in relation to surplus, wages, services and commodities which "easily superceded interactions on a village and family level. The enlarged scale of these interactions made the older means of interaction through habit and personal acquaintances inadequate. In other words, a more objective and impersonal system was needed in order ensure and guarantee that exchange would remain constant in every direction. This brought to the development of counting and measuring systems, and systems meant to give a concrete value to commodities, labour, time and land."[55] "To relieve the tedium of long calculations, the Mesopotamians made extensive use of mathematical tables. These included tables for finding reciprocals, squares, cubes, and square and cube roots, as well as exponential tables and even tables of values of n3 + n2, for which there is no modern equivalent."[56]

Most frequent collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ offering to the gods ♥ There was a long tradition of making regular offerings to the gods in Elam, with texts describing these offerings going as far back as the Late Awan Dynasty [202]. These offerings were usually performed either daily or monthly, and the items offered usually included wine, beer, grain, flour, and livestock (usually sheep and/or goats) [203]. Human sacrifice is never mentioned, and neither are offerings of currency. Often, texts describing these offerings are bureaucratic accounts of the exact quantities required of each resource offered [204]. Perhaps the fact that this ritual was performed regularly, as well as the fact that it was almost treated like a bureaucratic matter (the amounts of sheep, grains, etc. allocated to the gods each day/month), allow us to infer that it did not involve particularly dysphoric or euphoric elements. However, it is possible that the meat from sacrificed livestock, as well as the beer and wine that were also part of the offering, were consumed by the ritual specialists and/or other participants or audience members [205].
