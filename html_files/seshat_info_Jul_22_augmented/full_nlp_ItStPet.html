Largest scale collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ pilgrimage ♥ Pilgrimage was the largest-scale collective ritual during this period, although mass pilgrimage to Rome would become more frequent later in the Middle Ages. Pilgrims to Rome would visit the city's shrines, basilicas, and the catacombs of the Roman-era Christians. Graffitti evidence indicates that Anglo-Saxons were visiting Rome's ritual sites as early as 700.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_172_ItStPet] "Pilgrimages to the eternal city were never unpopular in the Middle Ages, although pilgrims were particularly numerous in certain periods. These pious journeys were in vogue between the eighth and mid-ninth centuries, when the route was relatively safe because of Frankish control of roads. [...] "Rome's greatest attractions were its extensive array of churches and the relics of saints they contained (see also Basilica of St John Lateran). The persecutions and martyrdoms of Christians that occurred in or were associated with Rome ensured its success as a goal for later Christian believers, who prized martyrdom (see alsoMartyrs) and wished to venerate the relics of martyrs for the faith. The relics of saints Peter and Paul were the primary aims of most pilgrims. These two figures became associated in the popular mind, and shared the same feast day (29 June). For this reason, a trip to Rome became known from the fifth century as a visit ad limina apostolorum ("to the thresholds of the Apostles"). Starting in the eighth century, Rome became known as a center for penitential pilgrimage, through which serious sins like homicide could be expiated. Some pilgrims petitioned directly to the pope to have their confessions heard and their sins forgiven. [...] "Pilgrims who went to Rome came from every corner of Western and Eastern Europe, and ranged from rulers (see also Royal Pilgrims) to paupers, encompassing laity, clergy, and the religious. They journeyed on the ancient roads that traversed the Italian peninsula; the well-traveled Via Francigena was a confluence of such paths. Trips to Rome presented particular challenges. A pilgrim approaching from the north would find the Alps impassable in the winter; and the city was infamous for malaria in the summer. The churches of Rome were spread out and some located in virtually deserted areas. Women pilgrims were barred from certain shrines starting in the fifteenth century, and possibly earlier. "[...]. "The problems posed by large crowds of pilgrims thronging to see important relics in Rome led to the construction of annular crypts in some churches, starting with St Peter. These permitted a large number of pilgrims to file by in an orderly fashion. Pilgrims could lower into the tombs cloths, which would touch the relics and retain the aura of the saint's holiness. The crowds of pilgrims also led to the creation of galleries for the overflow of pilgrims. "[...] "Medieval travelers, no less than modern ones, employed written guidebooks to orient them to the many churches in the eternal city. These range widely from simple lists of churches to visit to more extensive descriptions of the items churches contained and information on other sites in the city. Notable among these are the sixth-century Cymiteria totius urbis Romanae; the seventh-century guides Notitia ecclesiarum urbis Romae, De locis sanctis martyrum, and papyri from the cathedral treasury of Monza; the eighth-century Einsiedeln itinerary; the twelfth-century Mirabilia urbis Romae, which was wildly popular and frequently translated and augmented; the Itinerarium Malmesburiense, which was incorporated in the twelfth-century Gesta regum Anglorum of William of Malmesbury; and John Capgrave's The Solace of Pilgrims, written ca. 1451. "The popes had major roles in promoting pilgrimage to the city. They led frequent and dramatic liturgies (see also Liturgy and Pilgrimage) at different churches, which regularly attracted pilgrims. The pontiffs established xenodochia, institutions designed to host them and care for their needs. The popes were the primary benefactors of the churches of Rome, granting them gifts and seeing to their maintenance. "Foreign colonies of various northern European groups that would admit pilgrims of their nationality known as scolae existed near St Peter between the eighth and twelfth century. By the thirteenth century, a greater number of private establishments had grown up for pilgrims. ♠ Name ♣ baptism ♥ Baptism was the fundamental rite of initiation into Christianity going back to Augustine's time, but changed substantially during the early medieval period. The theology behind the ritual changed considerably; during this period there was not the emphasis that would later be placed on the need for children to be baptized to avoid damnation or consignment to Limbo.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_212_ItStPet] As Thompson phrased it, during the Middle Ages, "Christians were made, not born." Most frequent collective ritual of the official cult[edit]

♠ Name ♣ mass ♥ The Mass was the fundamental ritual of Christianity; indeed, as Arnold as argued, "...in whatever form, the mass can be seen as a key ritual in the production of medieval community...."[MAJIDBENAM_REF_256_ItStPet] There were many varieties of masses, varying in frequency and nature depending on the community involved. Monasteries and convents held several masses a day, in particular masses for the dead.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_257_ItStPet] Parish priests were supposed to say one mass daily, but it is unclear whether this was common, especially during this period, which predates the general establishment of parish churches in localities. High mass was rare, most likely restricted to high holidays such as Christmas; the laity were only expected to attend mass on Sundays, and even this was not asserted as a fundamental part of orthodox Christianity until a later period.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_258_ItStPet] A rough description of a typical mass is as follows. The priest read out a psalm on the nature of original sin, followed by hymns on Sundays. Then the priest read from the Gospel, and prepared the bread and wine for the Eucharist (at this point, the laity would give any offerings they had for the collection box). This description is from (Arnold)[MAJIDBENAM_REF_259_ItStPet] [MAJIDBENAM_REF_260_ItStPet] The priest then passed the Pax, an object with the crucifix on it, around and the laity kissed it.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_261_ItStPet] This seems to have taken the place of Communion on days when the laity did not participate in it; mass then ended. Monastic masses and special liturgical masses would have been slightly different, but these were the main elements. NOTE: Most Christians-in fact, probably the vast majority, during this period-did not attend mass on anything approaching a regular basis, perhaps at all.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_262_ItStPet] Urban people, in particular those in Rome, probably attended church more regularly than, say, those in the remote Appenines, but this is no more than a reasonable hypothesis for this period. I would also like to note the serious paucity of sources, and thus of secondary scholarship, on the early medieval mass in Italy: we simply do not know very much about lived religion in early medieval Europe, and especially in Italy.
