♠ Geometrical ♣ inferred present ♥ "[T]he origin of mathematics, and geometry in particular, in the Indian subcontinent may actually date back to the third millennium BCE and the Indus Valley civilization, rather than beginning with the Sulbasutras of the first millennium BCE as is conventionally thought. Although the well-planned cities, standardized system of measurements and evidence of long-range trade contacts with Mesopotamia and Central Asia attest to the high technological sophistication of this civilization, the unavailability of written records has up to now prevented us from acquiring a detailed understanding of the level of mathematical knowledge attained by the Indus people. This speaks of a fairly sophisticated knowledge of geometry that may have persisted in the local population long after the decline of the urban centers associated with the civilization and it may well have influenced in some part the mathematical thinking of the later Vedic culture of the first millennium BCE." However, more recent debate has highlighted regional differences that have qualified such assumptions. Nausharo, although located in Baluchistan, seems to be allied strongly to the Sindhi region and thus it is safe to discuss it together with finds from Mohenjo-daro.[MAJIDBENAM_BR]The nature of Indus religion has been elusive; and though much has been written on it the fact that John Marshall's description is still the standard work and he put great weight on mother goddess figurines and phallic symbols[223]. The main themes seem to be worship of the natural world, particularly the bull, and the use of water in ritual, including bathing.[224][MAJIDBENAM_BR]One of the main problems is that the script, virtually only found on the seals, has never been translated, despite numerous claims to the contrary[225]Credible evidence and a review of past efforts is in [226].[MAJIDBENAM_BR]The evidence falls mainly into a number of groups. The first consists of depictions of what could be gods in pictorial art, mainly seals. The most common seal type is the bull (which may have only one horn) standing over what appears to be a brazier. This makes up over half of all seals found. Other scenes include figures associated with a pipal tree (ficus religiosa), which is sacred in Indian religions (including Buddhism) even today[227] and whose leaves are a popular design on Harappan pottery. Given the importance of the sacred tree in later history it is probably safe to assign a similar importance in this period. Animals such as the tiger and the deer are also popular; and one seal appears to show animal figures on standards in a procession[228]. The most famous seal image is that of the Proto-Shiva, a figure in yogic posture who is wearing a bull headdress or is half-man half-bull. He possibly has three faces; and his other features are an erect phallus and multiple armlets. He has been interpreted as a shaman, but also as a forerunner of the Hindu god Shiva in the latter's role of Lord of the Beasts (Pashupati); for the seal also includes the tiger and other jungle creatures who surround him. Although a popular interpretation, it has been fairly rejected by a number of scholars[229][230].[MAJIDBENAM_BR]He could also be a priest, for a seal showing a collective ritual shows him bowing before a horned deity in a pipal tree, with other figures processing below them[231]. This and the procession are the two major images of ritual.[MAJIDBENAM_BR]The second type of evidence consists of figurines. These are found more in the south of the Indus (the Greater Indus Valley) than in other regions, where animal figurines are dominant[232]. The vast majority of human images are female with elaborate headdresses and jewellery. They have been interpreted as mother goddesses and fertility figures or as votive figurines; however, it is the men who are naked and with prominent genitals rather than the women[233]. They are found in domestic contexts suggesting household worship[234][235].[MAJIDBENAM_BR]There are very few sculptures. A "dancing girl" of bronze may or may not be involved in ritual. The "priest-king", interpreted as a theocratic figure, wears a robe decorated with trefoils, which may have some religious significance; but there is no justification for his title[236]. None of the other pieces is found in a context that makes it clear they are divine images.[MAJIDBENAM_BR]Most importantly, there are several great baths, the largest of which is at Mohenjo-daro. This points to rituals involving bathing[237]; and the placement of the Great Bath on the Citadel at Mohenjo-daro, with restricted access, points to some restriction in participation. At this point, however, even evidence for a specialist priesthood is not forthcoming. An urban civilisation, with high levels of economic organisation, there is not as much evidence for hierarchy as in other ancient civilisations; and although there is evidence of some[238] it is unclear how that impacted on ritual organisation.

Moreover, little is known about Harappan ritual specialists, "which may or may not have been present"[252]. On the other hand, Possehl also argues for the existence of "core beliefs" strongly held throughout the Indus Valley[253], which does suggest that someone was in charge of ensuring both orthodoxy and orthopraxy, at least in public contexts.

Headdresses made from the horns of a buffalo or less commonly of a bull were worn by figures that seem to represent deities in mythological scenes on seals and in other contexts. Buffalo horns, often with pendant stars or associated with pipal leaves or flowers, also appeared on pottery from the Early Harappan period onward. A jar from Padri in Gujarat is decorated with a buffalo horn motif and with a large figure in a ragged skirt wearing an enormous pair of buffalo horns. Buffaloes frequently appear in scenes on seals or tablets, often in combat with humans. In some, a figure wrestles one or two animals; in others, a man stands with one foot on the animal’s bowed head and pierces its body with a spear. Some terra-cotta figurines depict tigers or other felines, and tiger stripes are shown on some of the figures identified as deities. In some scenes a stick figure sits in a neem tree, watched by a prowling tiger looking back over its shoulder. It seems that bovids and tigers, magnificent and powerful creatures, played a major and balancing role in the iconography of the Indus civilization, both individually and in combination.
